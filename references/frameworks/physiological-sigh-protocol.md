## When to Use

Use this when someone needs to reduce stress or anxiety immediately — not in 10 minutes, not after a meditation session, but right now. This is the single fastest voluntary method known to shift the nervous system from sympathetic (fight-or-flight) to parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) activation. It works during conversations, in meetings, between exercise sets, during panic, or any moment when real-time calming is needed. It takes approximately 5 seconds and requires no equipment, no app, and no special environment.

Huberman calls this his most-recommended tool across all episodes. It appears in at least 8 separate episodes spanning breathing, fitness, stress, and mental health.

## The Protocol

The physiological sigh is a breathing pattern first identified by physiologists in the 1930s. It occurs spontaneously in all mammals approximately every 5 minutes during normal breathing, and more frequently during sleep and crying. The deliberate version harnesses the same mechanism on demand.

### Step 1: Double Inhale Through the Nose

Take a deep inhale through the nose. At the top of that inhale, before exhaling, sneak in a second, shorter inhale through the nose. This second inhale is critical — it reinflates collapsed alveoli (the tiny air sacs in the lungs), which maximizes the surface area available for gas exchange and increases the volume of carbon dioxide that can be offloaded on the exhale.

> "Two inhales through the nose and a long extended exhale through the mouth, the so-called physiological sigh not named by me but rather named by physiologists in the 1930s as far as we know, is the fastest way to shift your nervous system from so-called sympathetic drive to more parasympathetic drive, from a state of greater alertness to a state of greater calm." — Andrew Huberman, LYYyQcAJZfk.md

### Step 2: Long Extended Exhale Through the Mouth

Exhale slowly and completely through the mouth. The exhale should be significantly longer than both inhales combined. This is the step that does the actual calming work. The extended exhale increases intrathoracic pressure, which slows heart rate by facilitating venous blood return to the heart.

> "Physiological sighing seems to actually, where you have more time spent exhaling than inhaling, seem, and there's reason to believe that it induces parasympathetic activity because you're increasing pressure in the chest and therefore allowing the heart to slow down because blood is being returned to the atrium more easily." — Dr. David Spiegel, SOo4yNoaAoc.md

### Step 3: One Rep Is Sufficient

A single physiological sigh is enough for an immediate calming effect. You do not need to repeat it unless you want deeper calming. For acute stress (argument, panic, pre-presentation nerves), 1-3 reps is the typical dose.

For sustained calming, see the Cyclic Sighing Protocol (repeated physiological sighs for 5 minutes).

### The Mechanism

The double inhale reinflates collapsed alveoli, maximizing the lung surface area available to offload CO2. The extended exhale then removes that CO2 efficiently while simultaneously increasing intrathoracic pressure. This mechanical pressure change slows the heart via the vagus nerve. The entire effect is mechanical and reflexive — it does not depend on belief, relaxation, or mental focus.

> "It turns out we sigh about every five minutes. And I would encourage anyone who finds that to be an unbelievable fact is to lie down in a quiet room and just breathe normally." — Dr. Jack Feldman, Y3NUEPNZDMA.md

### When to Deploy It

- Between exercise sets (one sigh at the start of the rest period)
- Before a difficult conversation or presentation
- During a panic attack or acute anxiety spike
- While stuck in traffic or in a frustrating situation
- Any time heart rate or stress levels spike unexpectedly
- During rest periods in high-intensity training

### Caveats

- This is not a meditation practice. It is a real-time intervention tool.
- It works even during conversation or activity — no need to close your eyes or sit down.
- The mechanism is mechanical (lung and heart pressure dynamics), not placebo.
- For chronic stress or ongoing anxiety, combine with longer-duration protocols (cyclic sighing, NSDR, meditation).

## Example

A software engineer is about to present to the executive team. She feels her heart racing and palms sweating 2 minutes before the call starts. She performs one physiological sigh at her desk: deep inhale through the nose, short second inhale through the nose at the top, then a slow extended exhale through the mouth lasting about 4 seconds. Within 10-15 seconds, her heart rate drops noticeably. She does one more for good measure. The presentation begins with her nervous system measurably calmer than it was 30 seconds prior.

## Output

After performing 1-3 physiological sighs, a person should experience:
- Measurable reduction in heart rate within 10-30 seconds
- Reduced subjective sense of stress or agitation
- Shift from sympathetic to parasympathetic nervous system dominance
- Restored ability to think clearly and speak calmly
- No cognitive fog, drowsiness, or side effects (unlike longer breathing protocols)
