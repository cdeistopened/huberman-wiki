# Sleep Supplements Stack

## Huberman's Personal Stack

Huberman has stated he takes the following most nights, approximately **30–60 minutes before sleep:**

1. **Magnesium threonate** — 200–400 mg (of the compound, yielding ~48 mg elemental magnesium per 200 mg)
2. **Theanine** — 100–400 mg
3. **Apigenin** — 50 mg

He has also discussed glycine and inositol as useful additions.

## The Supplements in Detail

### Magnesium Threonate (200–400 mg)

**Why threonate specifically:** Magnesium threonate (also sold as Magtein) is the only form of magnesium shown to significantly cross the blood-brain barrier. Other forms (citrate, oxide, glycinate) provide systemic magnesium but don't concentrate in the brain the way threonate does.

**Mechanism:** Increases brain magnesium levels, which enhances GABA receptor activity (the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter), promotes neural quieting, and supports the transition from wakefulness to sleep.

**Dosage:** Most studies and supplement labels suggest 200–400 mg of magnesium threonate (not elemental magnesium — the threonate compound). Take 30–60 minutes before bed.

**Alternative:** If threonate is unavailable or too expensive, **magnesium bisglycinate** at 200 mg is the next best option. It has good bioavailability and mild calming effects, though it doesn't cross the blood-brain barrier as effectively.

### Theanine (100–400 mg)

**What it is:** An amino acid found naturally in tea leaves (especially green tea).

**Mechanism:** Increases alpha brain wave activity (associated with calm alertness), enhances GABA, and reduces excitatory glutamate signaling. It promotes relaxation without sedation.

**Dosage:** Start at 100 mg. Many people use 200–400 mg. Huberman has taken up to 400 mg.

**Important caveat:** Theanine can produce **vivid dreams** in some people. For some, this is pleasant; for others, it's disturbing or causes them to wake more often. If you experience excessively vivid or unpleasant dreams, discontinue theanine. Huberman has mentioned this multiple times — it's one of the most common reasons people drop theanine from the stack.

### Apigenin (50 mg)

**What it is:** A flavonoid found in chamomile. Concentrated chamomile extract, essentially.

**Mechanism:** Mild sedative effect through modulation of GABA-A receptors and reduction of cortisol. It's a gentle compound — not a knockout drug.

**Dosage:** 50 mg before bed. This is the dose Huberman uses.

**Note:** Apigenin has mild estrogen-modulating properties. Huberman has mentioned that men who are concerned about estrogenic effects might want to be cautious, though at 50 mg this is likely negligible. Women may actually benefit from its mild estrogenic activity.

### Glycine (2 g)

**Mechanism:** Glycine lowers **core body temperature** — and the drop in core body temperature is one of the primary signals that initiates sleep. It also acts as an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the CNS.

**Dosage:** 2 g (2,000 mg) before bed.

**Practical note:** Glycine is inexpensive and available as a powder with a slightly sweet taste. Can be mixed into water or taken as capsules.

### Inositol (900 mg)

**What it is:** A carbocyclic sugar involved in neurotransmitter signaling.

**Mechanism:** Supports serotonin receptor function. Serotonin is a precursor to melatonin, so supporting serotonin pathways indirectly supports the sleep-onset process. Also has anxiolytic (anti-anxiety) effects.

**Dosage:** 900 mg before bed. Huberman has mentioned he takes this.

**Additional benefit:** Inositol at higher doses (12–18 g) has been studied for OCD and anxiety. At the 900 mg sleep dose, the effect is mild but contributes to the stack.

## Why NOT Melatonin

Huberman has been outspoken about melatonin misuse:

- **Melatonin is a hormone, not a supplement.** It's involved in puberty, reproductive function, and circadian regulation.
- Most commercial melatonin products contain **far more than the labeled dose** — independent testing has found some products contain 4–5x the stated amount.
- Regular exogenous melatonin can **suppress the body's own melatonin production** and may affect reproductive hormones (LH, FSH, testosterone, estrogen).
- The only appropriate use Huberman endorses: **jet lag**, at **0.5–1 mg** (not the 5–10 mg tablets commonly sold), taken at the target bedtime in the new time zone for 2–3 nights.

## Stacking Strategy

The full stack doesn't need to be taken every night. A reasonable approach:

| Level | What to Take |
|-------|-------------|
| Core (nightly) | Magnesium threonate + apigenin |
| Enhanced (most nights) | Add theanine (if dreams aren't an issue) |
| Full stack | Add glycine + inositol |
| Jet lag only | Add 0.5–1 mg melatonin for 2–3 nights |

## Caveats

- Supplements are not FDA-regulated. Use brands that do third-party testing (NSF, USP, Informed Sport).
- These are sleep *support* tools — they do not override poor sleep hygiene (late screens, irregular schedule, caffeine timing).
- Magnesium can cause loose stools at higher doses. Start low.
- Theanine may not be appropriate for people on anti-anxiety medication — consult a physician.
- None of these replace clinical treatment for insomnia. If you consistently can't sleep, see a sleep specialist.
