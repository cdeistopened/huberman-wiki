## When to Use

Use this framework when you want to understand why some achievements feel hollow while unexpected small wins feel disproportionately good. Use it to design better habit loops, understand why social media is so addictive, diagnose why a goal you worked toward for months produced no satisfaction when you reached it, or understand the neurochemical basis of addiction and craving.

## The Framework

Reward prediction error (RPE) is the brain's core satisfaction algorithm. It is not the reward itself that determines how you feel — it is the gap between what you expected and what you got.

**The equation:** Dopamine response = Actual reward experienced - Reward predicted

This single computation governs whether you feel elated, neutral, or disappointed after any pursuit.

### The Three Outcomes

1. **Reward > Expectation:** Large dopamine peak. The behavior is strongly reinforced. You want to do it again. This is the feeling of a pleasant surprise, an unexpected compliment, or a windfall.

2. **Reward = Expectation:** Dopamine returns to baseline. Neutral. The behavior is maintained but not reinforced. This is why the tenth time you eat at a favorite restaurant feels less special than the first.

3. **Reward < Expectation:** Dopamine drops below baseline. Disappointment. The behavior is less likely to be repeated. This is the feeling of working hard for a promotion and receiving a lateral move instead.

### The Desire-Pursuit-Reward Chain

RPE does not only operate at the moment of reward. The dopamine system tracks the entire contingency chain:

1. **Desire** for something triggers an initial dopamine peak (wanting)
2. That peak drops below baseline, creating a motivational trough — the discomfort of not having the thing
3. **Pursuit** begins, driven by the trough. The brain monitors all environmental cues during pursuit (signs of progress or setback)
4. **Reward** arrives (or doesn't). The RPE calculation runs against the full prediction built during the pursuit phase

> "Craving for things is not just about craving for those things per se; it's also a desire to relieve the pain of not having those things." — Andrew Huberman

This means craving has a dual nature: you are simultaneously pulled toward the reward AND pushed away from the discomfort of the trough. The trough itself is motivating — but it is experienced as pain.

> "Dopamine is released in anticipation of what we want... desire and the need to move in order to pursue and reach goals are one and the same process." — Andrew Huberman

### Implications for Habits

**Habits that stick** exploit positive RPE: variable rewards (sometimes better than expected) sustain engagement because the brain cannot build an accurate prediction model. This is why intermittent reinforcement schedules — slot machines, social media notifications, variable-quality content feeds — are so powerful.

**Habits that fade** suffer from zero or negative RPE: once the brain accurately predicts the reward, dopamine returns to baseline upon receipt. The behavior loses its motivational pull. This is why routine exercise programs become stale, why the third week of a new diet feels harder than the first, and why relationships lose their initial spark.

**Habits that harm** create massive positive RPE followed by catastrophic troughs: drugs of abuse produce rewards so far above prediction that the subsequent trough is unbearable, driving compulsive repetition. The prediction ratchets up with each use, requiring larger doses for the same RPE.

### The Prefrontal Override

RPE operates subconsciously — you experience it as context-dependent craving and motivation, not as a calculation. However, the prefrontal cortex provides context-setting that can modulate RPE. This is the neurochemical basis for cognitive reframing: by changing your conscious interpretation of an experience, you can shift the prediction model and alter the dopamine response.

This is why the growth mindset reframe works: by attaching reward to effort rather than outcome, you change what the brain predicts, which changes the RPE calculation for every moment of the process.

## Example

A startup founder spends 18 months building toward a Series A. She visualizes the moment of signing the term sheet daily. When the round closes at exactly the valuation she targeted, she feels... flat. Not elated. Not even particularly satisfied. Her partner is confused — this was the goal.

RPE explains the flatness: reward equaled prediction. The 18 months of visualization built a precise prediction model. When reality matched the model exactly, the RPE was zero. Dopamine returned to baseline. No surge of satisfaction.

Compare this to a founder who expected the round to fall through and got a surprise term sheet at 2x her ask. The RPE is massively positive. She feels euphoric — not because the objective outcome was better, but because the gap between prediction and reality was larger.

The practical lesson: if you want achievements to feel satisfying, either lower your expectations (difficult and possibly counterproductive) or learn to attach dopamine to the effort phase rather than the outcome (the growth mindset reframe).

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

- Diagnose why specific achievements felt hollow (reward matched prediction; RPE was zero)
- Understand why variable reward schedules are more motivating than fixed ones
- Design habit systems that maintain positive RPE through strategic variability
- Recognize when craving is being driven by the pain of the trough rather than genuine desire for the reward
- Apply the prefrontal override to shift your prediction model and change your dopamine response to effort
