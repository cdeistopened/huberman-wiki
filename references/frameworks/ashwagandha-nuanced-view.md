## When to Use

Apply this framework when someone asks about ashwagandha supplementation, cortisol reduction through supplements, adaptogens for stress management, or evening relaxation protocols. Also relevant when someone mentions taking ashwagandha in the morning (which Huberman specifically advises against at therapeutic doses) or asks about cycling ashwagandha.

## The Recommendation

Ashwagandha is one of the most effective supplemental cortisol reducers available -- studies show reductions of 11-29%. But Huberman's recommendation is heavily conditional. The timing matters enormously, and behavioral interventions should always come first.

The core rule: ashwagandha at therapeutic doses (300+ mg) should only be taken in the evening. Taking it in the morning would blunt the natural cortisol peak that drives alertness, focus, and immune function in the first half of the day. This is one of the most common mistakes people make with ashwagandha -- they take it in the morning because they feel stressed, but they are undermining the exact hormonal signal their body needs.

Small amounts of ashwagandha (under 50-100 mg), such as those found in blended products like AG1, are not a concern in the morning. They fall below the threshold that meaningfully suppresses cortisol.

Huberman takes 300 mg in the evening and recommends starting there rather than escalating to higher doses. The principle: always seek the minimal effective dose.

> "If you look at the literature on ashwagandha, you will see that indeed it can be effective in reducing cortisol levels by anywhere from about 11% to 29%." -- Huberman

> "Restrict your intake of ashwagandha to the late afternoon and evening and nighttime hours to keep your cortisol levels low and avoid taking dosages of ashwagandha certainly as high as 300 milligrams anytime early in the day." -- Huberman

> "Anything less than say 50 or 100 milligrams early in the day is not going to have a significant impact on cortisol." -- Huberman

## Caveats

- **Timing is non-negotiable at therapeutic doses.** Taking 300+ mg of ashwagandha in the morning will suppress the cortisol peak that is essential for wakefulness, immune function, and focus. This is the most important point in the entire framework.
- **Behavioral tools come first.** Huberman emphasizes dimming lights in the evening, reducing stress exposure, and using other behavioral interventions before adding ashwagandha. Supplementation should complement behavior, not replace it.
- **Thyroid interactions.** While not explicitly discussed in the cortisol episode transcripts, ashwagandha's effects on thyroid function are widely documented elsewhere. People with thyroid conditions or on thyroid medication should consult their physician. Ashwagandha can increase thyroid hormone levels, which may be beneficial for some (hypothyroid) but problematic for others (hyperthyroid or those on thyroid replacement therapy).
- **Cycling consideration.** Though Huberman does not specify a cycling protocol in these episodes, many practitioners recommend cycling ashwagandha (e.g., 8 weeks on, 2-4 weeks off) to prevent adaptation and to monitor for thyroid changes.
- **The cortisol-lowering effect is more dramatic than apigenin** (another cortisol reducer in Huberman's toolkit), which is why timing discipline matters more with ashwagandha.
- **Not a sleep supplement per se.** Ashwagandha reduces cortisol, which indirectly supports sleep preparation, but it is mechanistically distinct from the magnesium-apigenin-theanine sleep stack.

## Output

When recommending ashwagandha, always specify:
1. Evening-only timing at therapeutic doses (300+ mg)
2. The cortisol reduction range (11-29%) and why that matters
3. That behavioral interventions should be tried first
4. The thyroid concern and recommendation to consult a physician
5. That small amounts in blended morning products are not a concern
