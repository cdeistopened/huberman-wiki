# Creatine for Brain Function

## The Protocol

- **Dosage:** 5 g daily of **creatine monohydrate**
- **Timing:** Doesn't matter — just take it consistently (with food is fine)
- **Form:** Monohydrate is the most studied and most cost-effective. Ignore "buffered," "HCl," or "ethyl ester" marketing.
- **Loading phase:** Not necessary. Daily 5 g reaches tissue saturation within 2–4 weeks. Loading (20 g/day for 5–7 days) just gets you there faster.

## Not Just for Muscles

Creatine is one of the most researched supplements in existence — over 500 peer-reviewed studies. Most of this research is on athletic performance, but a growing body of evidence shows significant **cognitive benefits.**

The brain represents roughly **2% of body mass** but uses approximately **20% of the body's energy**. The brain's energy demands are enormous and constant, making it highly sensitive to disruptions in ATP (adenosine triphosphate) recycling.

## The Mechanism: Phosphocreatine Recycling

Here's how creatine works at the cellular level:

1. Your cells use ATP for energy. When ATP gives up a phosphate group to power a reaction, it becomes ADP (adenosine diphosphate).
2. **Phosphocreatine** donates its phosphate group back to ADP, regenerating ATP. This is the fastest way your cells can recycle energy.
3. Creatine supplementation increases the **phosphocreatine reservoir** in both muscle and brain tissue.
4. A larger reservoir = faster and more sustained ATP recycling = better performance under demand.

In the brain, this translates to:

- Better **working memory** — the mental "RAM" you use for reasoning, planning, and holding information
- Reduced **mental fatigue** — sustained cognitive output during long work sessions
- Improved performance under **sleep deprivation** — creatine partially buffers the cognitive decline caused by poor sleep
- Faster **processing speed** in tasks requiring executive function

## The Evidence

Key studies Huberman has referenced:

- **Sleep deprivation study:** Creatine supplementation (compared to placebo) significantly reduced the cognitive impairment caused by 24 hours of sleep deprivation. Subjects performed better on executive function, reaction time, and mood measures.
- **Working memory study:** 5 g/day creatine for 6 weeks improved performance on backward digit span and Ravens Progressive Matrices (tests of working memory and fluid intelligence).
- **Vegetarian advantage:** Vegetarians and vegans show larger cognitive benefits from creatine supplementation, likely because they have lower baseline creatine stores (meat is the primary dietary source of creatine).
- **TBI research:** Preliminary evidence suggests creatine may be neuroprotective — supporting recovery from traumatic brain injury by maintaining cellular energy supply during metabolic crisis.

## Saturation, Not Timing

Creatine works through **tissue saturation**, not acute dosing:

- Taking 5 g before a workout doesn't give you a mental boost 30 minutes later
- The benefit comes from maintaining a consistently elevated phosphocreatine pool over weeks and months
- Skipping a day occasionally is fine — the reservoir depletes slowly
- Sporadic use (taking it only on "hard" days) does not work — the reservoir needs to be maintained

This is why timing doesn't matter. Take it with your morning coffee, in a post-workout shake, with dinner — whatever helps you be consistent.

## Practical Notes

- **Creatine monohydrate is cheap** — roughly $0.05–0.10 per 5 g serving from reputable brands
- **Micronized monohydrate** dissolves better in water but is chemically identical
- Mix into water, coffee, a smoothie, or any liquid. It's tasteless and odorless in small doses.
- **Water retention:** Creatine draws water into muscle cells. Expect 1–3 lbs of water weight gain in the first 1–2 weeks. This is intracellular water, not bloating, and stabilizes.
- Stay well-hydrated when supplementing creatine (but you should be doing that anyway)

## Caveats

- **Kidney concerns:** The old myth that creatine damages kidneys has been thoroughly debunked in healthy individuals. However, if you have pre-existing kidney disease, consult your nephrologist.
- **Hair loss concern:** One study (from 2009, rugby players) found elevated DHT levels with creatine loading. This has not been replicated, and most experts consider the evidence insufficient to conclude creatine causes hair loss. However, if you're genetically predisposed to male pattern baldness and concerned, discuss with your dermatologist.
- **Adolescents:** Generally considered safe, but pediatric data is limited. The American Academy of Pediatrics neither endorses nor prohibits creatine use in adolescent athletes.
- **GI discomfort:** Some people experience stomach upset at 5 g. Splitting into two 2.5 g doses (morning and evening) can help. Taking with food also reduces GI issues.
