## When to Use

Apply this framework when someone asks about magnesium supplementation, wants to know the difference between magnesium forms, or is trying to choose between threonate, bisglycinate, citrate, or other magnesium compounds. Also relevant when someone reports sleep difficulties, nighttime restlessness, or wants to support cognitive function through supplementation.

## The Recommendation

Not all magnesium is the same. The form determines where it acts in the body, and choosing the wrong form means paying for a supplement that does not reach its intended target. Huberman has taken magnesium threonate nightly for over eight years and considers form selection one of the most important decisions in magnesium supplementation.

**Magnesium L-Threonate** is Huberman's primary recommendation. It crosses the blood-brain barrier more effectively than other forms, which makes it the preferred choice for sleep support, cognitive function, and cortisol modulation. It is the core magnesium in his sleep stack.

**Magnesium Bisglycinate (Glycinate)** is interchangeable with threonate for sleep purposes, though Huberman considers threonate "better for a variety of reasons." Bisglycinate is gentler on the GI tract and a reasonable alternative for the roughly 5% of people who experience digestive distress from threonate.

**Magnesium Citrate** is primarily useful for its laxative effect and GI motility. It does not cross the blood-brain barrier as effectively as threonate. While it raises systemic magnesium levels, it is not the right choice if the goal is sleep or cognitive support.

**Magnesium Oxide** is the cheapest and most common form found in grocery-store supplements. It has poor bioavailability and is largely excreted without absorption. Huberman does not recommend it.

> "145 milligrams of magnesium threonate. 50 milligrams of apigenin. And 100 to 400 milligrams of theanine taken, again, alone or in combination with the other supplements mentioned in the stack many people find allows them to get really drowsy and fall asleep, sleep really deeply, and they feel much more refreshed the next day and they don't have a grogginess to them." -- Huberman

> "There's evidence that magnesium L-threonate can indirectly activate pathways that might further help suppress cortisol." -- Huberman

## Dosage & Timing

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Recommended form** | Magnesium L-threonate (primary) or magnesium bisglycinate (alternative) |
| **Dosage** | 145 mg of magnesium threonate |
| **Timing** | 30-60 minutes before bedtime |
| **Stomach** | Works best on a relatively empty stomach (ideally no food for ~3 hours); still effective after late meals |
| **Cycling** | None required; Huberman takes it every night |
| **Onset** | Effects typically felt within 20-40 minutes |

## Caveats

- Approximately 5% of people experience GI distress (diarrhea) from magnesium threonate. Huberman is direct about this: "The proper dosage for you, in other words, would be zero milligrams." If this happens, switch to bisglycinate.
- Start with single-ingredient formulations rather than blended magnesium products. This allows you to identify which form your body tolerates and to adjust dosages independently.
- Magnesium citrate will not deliver the same cognitive or sleep benefits as threonate -- it works primarily on the gut, not the brain.
- Consult a physician before adding any supplement, particularly if you are on medications that affect electrolyte balance.
- The 145 mg dose refers to elemental magnesium from threonate, not the total weight of the compound. Check labels carefully.

## Output

When recommending magnesium, always specify:
1. The exact form (threonate, bisglycinate, or citrate) matched to the user's goal
2. The dosage in milligrams of elemental magnesium
3. The timing relative to bedtime or meals
4. Whether the user falls in the ~5% who should avoid threonate
5. The distinction between brain-penetrating forms (threonate) and systemic forms (citrate)
