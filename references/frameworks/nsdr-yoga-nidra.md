# NSDR (Non-Sleep Deep Rest) / Yoga Nidra

## The Protocol

1. Lie down in a comfortable position (on your back is ideal)
2. Close your eyes
3. Follow a guided body scan — systematically bring attention to each body part, from toes to head
4. Breathe with long, slow exhales (but don't force a rigid pattern)
5. Stay still — do not move or adjust position
6. Duration: **10–20 minutes** (even 10 minutes is effective)
7. You may feel like you're drifting toward sleep but not quite falling asleep — this is the target state

## What NSDR Actually Is

NSDR is Huberman's rebranding of **yoga nidra** — a practice with thousands of years of history in the yogic tradition. Huberman coined the term "Non-Sleep Deep Rest" to make it accessible to people who would never try something called "yoga nidra" and to distinguish it from meditation.

The practice takes you into a state between waking and sleeping — similar to the **hypnagogic state** you pass through as you fall asleep. Brain activity shifts from beta waves (normal waking) into alpha and theta wave patterns, which are associated with deep relaxation, creativity, and memory consolidation.

**This is not meditation.** You don't need to "empty your mind," maintain focus on a mantra, or cultivate a particular mental state. You just lie still and follow instructions. If your mind wanders, that's fine. The protocol works regardless of your subjective experience during the session.

## The Evidence

### Dopamine

A study from a Scandinavian research group found that yoga nidra practice increased **striatal dopamine release by up to 65%**. This is significant because:

- Dopamine is not just about pleasure — it's about motivation, readiness, and drive
- The increase came from a resting protocol, not a stimulant
- The dopamine increase was accompanied by reduced cortisol

This makes NSDR one of the few practices that raises dopamine without a subsequent crash below baseline (unlike caffeine, stimulants, or social media).

### Learning and Memory

Research on napping and rest states shows that **deliberate rest immediately after a learning session** accelerates memory consolidation. The brain replays and consolidates newly learned material during these rest states — similar to what happens during sleep, but achievable in 10–20 minutes during the day.

Huberman has recommended doing an NSDR session after studying or practicing a new skill to enhance retention.

### Sleep Replacement

NSDR does not replace sleep — nothing does. However, it can partially compensate for lost sleep:

- After a poor night of sleep, a 10–20 minute NSDR session restores some of the subjective alertness and cognitive capacity that was lost
- It reduces the adenosine-driven sleep pressure that accumulates during wakefulness
- Huberman has stated he uses NSDR to recover from nights where he slept less than 6 hours

## When to Use NSDR

| Scenario | Timing | Duration |
|----------|--------|----------|
| Afternoon energy dip | 1:00–3:00 PM | 10–20 min |
| After poor night's sleep | Morning or early afternoon | 20 min |
| After intense learning | Immediately after study session | 10 min |
| Before a demanding event | 30–60 min before | 10–20 min |
| General stress management | Any time | 10 min |

## How to Do It

Huberman recommends using a **guided audio track** rather than trying to do it on your own, especially when starting out. He has a free NSDR script on YouTube (search "Huberman NSDR" — the 10-minute version is the most widely used).

Other options:
- The Reveri app (developed by David Spiegel at Stanford, Huberman has endorsed it)
- Any yoga nidra recording on YouTube or Insight Timer
- The key elements are: body scan + long exhales + stillness + 10–20 min duration

## NSDR vs. Meditation vs. Napping

| Feature | NSDR | Meditation | Napping |
|---------|------|------------|---------|
| Effort required | Very low | Moderate to high | None |
| Risk of grogginess | None | None | High (if >20 min) |
| Dopamine increase | Yes (65% in one study) | Variable | Minimal data |
| Learning consolidation | Yes | Some evidence | Yes |
| Requires practice/training | No | Yes | No |
| Time to benefit | First session | Weeks to months | First session |

## Caveats

- The 65% dopamine figure comes from a small study and should be taken as directional, not definitive.
- Some people fall fully asleep during NSDR. This is fine occasionally, but the target is the state *between* waking and sleep. If you consistently fall asleep, you likely need more actual sleep.
- NSDR is not a substitute for treating clinical insomnia or sleep disorders.
- If you have trauma that surfaces during body scan practices, work with a clinician who understands somatic approaches.
