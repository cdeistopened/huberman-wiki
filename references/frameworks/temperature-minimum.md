# Temperature Minimum

## The Core Concept

Your body temperature follows a predictable 24-hour cycle. It rises during the day, peaks in the late afternoon, and drops through the evening and night. The lowest point — your **temperature minimum** — occurs approximately **2 hours before your natural wake time.**

If you naturally wake at 6:00 AM, your temperature minimum is around 4:00 AM.
If you naturally wake at 8:00 AM, your temperature minimum is around 6:00 AM.

This temperature minimum is your **circadian anchor point.** It determines how your body responds to light, exercise, and food as clock-shifting signals.

## The Rule: Before vs. After Temperature Minimum

Light exposure (and to a lesser extent, exercise and eating) shifts your circadian clock differently depending on whether it occurs before or after your temperature minimum:

### Light BEFORE Temperature Minimum → Clock Shifts LATER

If you're exposed to bright light in the hours before your temperature minimum (e.g., 1:00–4:00 AM for a 6:00 AM waker), your body interprets this as "the day is still going" and delays your clock. You'll want to go to sleep later and wake up later.

### Light AFTER Temperature Minimum → Clock Shifts EARLIER

If you're exposed to bright light in the hours after your temperature minimum (e.g., 5:00–9:00 AM for a 6:00 AM waker), your body interprets this as "morning is here" and advances your clock. You'll want to go to sleep earlier and wake up earlier.

This is why **morning sunlight viewing works for most people:** sunrise typically occurs after the temperature minimum, so it advances the clock, making you feel alert earlier in the morning and sleepy earlier at night.

And it's why **bright light at night is so disruptive:** for most people, late-night light falls in the phase-delay window (before the next morning's temperature minimum), pushing the clock later — making it harder to wake up the next morning.

## Practical Application: Jet Lag

This is the most powerful practical use of the temperature minimum concept. Here's the protocol:

### Step 1: Determine Your Temperature Minimum in Your Home Time Zone

Take your typical natural wake time and subtract 2 hours.

- Wake at 6 AM → temp min at 4 AM
- Wake at 7 AM → temp min at 5 AM
- Wake at 8 AM → temp min at 6 AM

### Step 2: Convert to Destination Time Zone

If you're flying from New York (wake at 6 AM, temp min at 4 AM EST) to London (+5 hours), your temperature minimum on arrival is 9:00 AM London time.

### Step 3: Use Light Strategically

**To advance your clock (traveling east — you need to wake up earlier):**
- Seek bright light AFTER your temperature minimum (in destination time)
- Avoid light BEFORE your temperature minimum
- In the London example: seek bright light from 9:00 AM onward, avoid bright light before 9:00 AM on the first day
- Each day, your temperature minimum will shift earlier as your clock adjusts (~1 hour per day)

**To delay your clock (traveling west — you need to stay up later):**
- Seek bright light BEFORE your temperature minimum (in destination time)
- Avoid light AFTER your temperature minimum in the early adjustment period
- This is usually easier because you're extending your day rather than compressing it

### Step 4: Shift Gradually

The circadian clock shifts approximately **1 hour per day** for eastward travel and **1.5 hours per day** for westward travel. Plan your light exposure accordingly over multiple days.

For trips crossing more than 6 time zones, shift partially before departure by adjusting your light exposure and sleep schedule 1–2 hours per day for several days before travel.

## Exercise Follows the Same Rules

Exercise raises core body temperature and acts as a clock signal (a "zeitgeber"):

- **Exercise BEFORE temperature minimum:** Shifts clock later (delays)
- **Exercise AFTER temperature minimum:** Shifts clock earlier (advances)

This is why morning exercise helps early risers maintain their schedule, and why late-night intense exercise can make it harder to fall asleep — it shifts the clock later.

## Food and Temperature Minimum

Eating raises core body temperature (thermic effect of food). The same before/after rules apply, though the effect is weaker than light. Eating a large meal very late at night is a mild phase-delaying signal.

## Why Morning Sunlight Is So Effective

For the vast majority of people:

1. Sunrise occurs AFTER the temperature minimum
2. Therefore, morning sunlight exposure provides a clock-advancing signal
3. This tightens the circadian rhythm — you feel alert during the day and sleepy at night at consistent times
4. The effect is cumulative and self-reinforcing over days

People who never get morning sunlight lose this daily calibration signal. Their clock drifts, leading to inconsistent sleep onset, morning grogginess, and evening alertness (the "night owl" drift that is often circadian misalignment rather than chronotype).

## Measuring Your Temperature Minimum

You don't need to measure it directly. For most purposes:

- Use your natural wake time (on a day with no alarm) minus 2 hours
- If you're using a wearable that tracks skin temperature (Oura, WHOOP, Apple Watch), you can observe the nightly temperature trough to refine your estimate
- Core body temperature and skin temperature are not identical, but the timing of the minimum is closely correlated

## Caveats

- The "2 hours before wake time" estimate assumes a regular sleep schedule. People with highly irregular sleep times may have a temperature minimum that's harder to predict.
- Shift workers face unique challenges — their temperature minimum may not align with societal day/night patterns. Specific shift-work protocols exist that use this framework.
- Individual chronotype (genetic tendency toward early or late sleep) shifts the temperature minimum. True "night owls" may have a later temperature minimum than the formula predicts.
- Blind individuals rely on non-photic zeitgebers (exercise, meals, social cues) since they cannot use light to calibrate. Melatonin at low doses can serve as an alternative clock signal.
- This is a simplified model. The full circadian system involves multiple oscillators, not just the SCN, and they can become desynchronized (social jet lag, shift work, irregular eating).
