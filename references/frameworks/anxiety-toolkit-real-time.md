## When to Use

Use this when someone is experiencing anxiety right now — or wants a layered system for managing anxiety in real time as it escalates. Most people know one or two anxiety tools but do not have a system that scales with intensity. This framework organizes Huberman's anxiety tools into an escalation ladder: the mildest intervention first, progressing to stronger interventions only as needed. It also provides the theoretical framework (interoception vs. exteroception) that explains why anxiety tools work and why some make anxiety worse.

## The Protocol

### The Core Framework: Interoception vs. Exteroception

Anxiety is fundamentally an interoceptive bias — the mind becomes excessively focused on internal signals (heartbeat, breathing rate, gut sensations, body temperature, racing thoughts). The anxious mind is "tuned inward" and interprets internal signals as threats.

All effective real-time anxiety tools work by shifting attention from interoception (internal) to exteroception (external). The more anxious someone is, the stronger the exteroceptive intervention needs to be.

> "If you're somebody who has a tremendous amount of interoceptive awareness, well, then meditating on your internal state may not be good, and actually, there's some evidence that it may actually be bad." — Andrew Huberman, wTBSGgbIvsY.md

This is why eyes-closed, breath-focused meditation can worsen anxiety in anxious people — it increases interoceptive load on a system that is already interoceptively overloaded.

### The Escalation Ladder

Use Level 1 first. If it does not bring anxiety below a manageable threshold within 30-60 seconds, move to Level 2. Continue escalating as needed.

---

**Level 1: Physiological Sigh (5 seconds, anywhere)**

The fastest voluntary intervention. Use for mild-to-moderate anxiety spikes.

1. Deep inhale through the nose
2. Second short inhale through the nose at the top
3. Long extended exhale through the mouth
4. 1-3 reps

This mechanically slows heart rate by increasing intrathoracic pressure. It does not require you to close your eyes, sit down, or stop what you are doing. Works during conversations, presentations, or any activity.

> "Two inhales through the nose and a long extended exhale through the mouth... is the fastest way to shift your nervous system from so-called sympathetic drive to more parasympathetic drive." — Andrew Huberman, LYYyQcAJZfk.md

---

**Level 2: Panoramic Vision (30 seconds - 2 minutes, needs visual space)**

If the physiological sigh reduces but does not resolve the anxiety, add visual field expansion.

1. Soften your gaze — stop focusing on any single point
2. Allow peripheral vision to expand so you are taking in the entire visual scene
3. Maintain this panoramic gaze for 30 seconds to 2 minutes
4. If possible, look at a distant point (horizon, far wall) to further shift toward exteroception

This directly counteracts the tunnel vision that accompanies sympathetic activation. Each visual distance corresponds to a different time-perception mode: close focus = fine-slicing time (anxious, hypervigilant); far focus = broader time bins (calm, spacious).

---

**Level 3: Optic Flow Walking (5-30 minutes, needs space to walk)**

If Levels 1 and 2 are insufficient, add forward movement.

1. Walk at a comfortable pace while maintaining panoramic vision
2. The forward movement generates optic flow (the visual scene streaming past you)
3. Optic flow has been shown to directly reduce amygdala activation
4. Continue for 5-30 minutes

This is why walks reliably reduce anxiety — it is not just the exercise component but the optic flow that is doing neurological work. The combination of panoramic vision + forward movement is a compound exteroceptive intervention.

---

**Level 4: Cyclic Sighing (5 minutes, needs to sit or lie down)**

For sustained or severe anxiety that walking has not resolved.

1. Sit or lie comfortably
2. Perform repeated physiological sighs (double inhale nose, long exhale mouth)
3. Continue for 5 minutes
4. All exhales longer and more vigorous than inhales

This is the protocol that won Huberman's head-to-head study against box breathing, cyclic hyperventilation, and meditation for reducing resting heart rate and improving mood 24 hours later.

---

**Level 5: NSDR / Yoga Nidra (10-30 minutes, needs to lie down)**

For severe anxiety or anxiety-induced exhaustion. This is a defocusing practice, not a focus practice — it reduces interoceptive load by deliberately letting attention drift.

1. Lie down, close eyes
2. Follow a guided body scan (progressive relaxation through body parts)
3. Deliberately defocus attention — do not concentrate on anything
4. Duration: 10, 20, or 30 minutes
5. Free scripts: search "NSDR Huberman" on YouTube

NSDR replenishes dopamine (striatal dopamine increase shown in a 30-minute yoga nidra study) and reduces cortisol. It is particularly effective for anxiety-related exhaustion — the state of being "wired but tired."

---

**Level 6: Self-Hypnosis (10-15 minutes, needs quiet space)**

For treatment-resistant or chronic anxiety. Requires prior assessment of hypnotizability (Spiegel Eye Roll Test). Approximately two-thirds of adults are hypnotizable.

1. Use the Reveri app or a trained clinician
2. Hypnotic induction: look up, close eyes, deep breath, exhale, body floating visualization
3. 10-15 minute sessions; 1-minute refreshers for maintenance
4. Produces specific brain circuit changes: reduced dorsal anterior cingulate activity, increased DLPFC-insula connectivity

> "The issue is control. And hypnosis, which has this terrible reputation of taking away control, is actually a superb way of enhancing your control over mind and body." — Dr. David Spiegel, SOo4yNoaAoc.md

### What NOT to Do During Anxiety

- Do NOT close your eyes and focus on your breathing if you are interoceptively biased (this increases the anxious signal)
- Do NOT try to "think your way out" of anxiety (cognitive override is weak when the sympathetic system is activated)
- Do NOT consume caffeine (amplifies sympathetic activation)
- Do NOT do cyclic hyperventilation or Wim Hof-style breathing (deliberately raises stress)
- Do NOT doom-scroll or check news (combines interoceptive focus with threat-detection content)

### Caveats

- Severe anxiety disorders (panic disorder, PTSD, OCD) require professional treatment — these tools complement clinical care, they do not replace it
- The physiological sigh works for nearly everyone. Higher-level interventions have more individual variability.
- Cyclic hyperventilation is a stress inoculation tool, NOT an anxiety management tool — it deliberately increases stress and should never be used during an anxiety episode
- People who are chronically anxious may need to rebuild their exteroceptive capacity gradually, starting with Level 2 (panoramic vision) as a daily practice even when not anxious

## Example

A 26-year-old man is at a work conference and feels a wave of anxiety building during a networking session. He starts with Level 1: one physiological sigh at the cocktail table. The edge comes off but anxiety persists. He shifts to Level 2: while appearing to listen to the conversation, he softens his gaze and takes in the full room with peripheral vision. The tunnel vision breaks and anxiety drops further. Still elevated, he excuses himself and takes a 10-minute walk around the building (Level 3), maintaining panoramic vision. By the time he returns, his autonomic state has reset. He did not need Levels 4-6. The entire intervention took 12 minutes and was invisible to everyone around him.

## Output

After applying the appropriate level of the escalation ladder, a person should experience:
- Measurable reduction in heart rate and subjective anxiety within the timeframe of the chosen level (5 seconds for Level 1, up to 30 minutes for Levels 5-6)
- Shift from interoceptive bias (internal focus, body scanning, threat detection) to exteroceptive engagement (environmental awareness, broader perspective)
- Restored cognitive function — anxiety degrades working memory and decision-making, and these tools reverse that degradation
- A repeatable, scalable system that matches tool intensity to anxiety intensity
