## When to Use

Use this framework when you notice your motivation, drive, or enjoyment of activities has changed — either acutely (you felt great this morning and now feel flat) or chronically (activities you used to love feel dull). This is the master framework for understanding dopamine. Every other dopamine protocol in the Huberman archive builds on this model. If you only learn one thing about dopamine, learn this.

## The Framework

Dopamine operates as a dynamic system with three interrelated components: **baseline**, **peaks**, and **troughs**. Huberman uses the analogy of a wave pool to make this tangible.

### The Wave Pool

Your dopamine baseline is the water level in a wave pool. Peaks are the waves. Troughs are the dips below the waterline that follow every wave. The critical insight: the water used to make the wave comes from the pool itself. Every peak temporarily depletes the pool.

> "Your experience of life and your level of motivation and drive depends on how much dopamine you have relative to your recent experience." — Andrew Huberman

### Baseline: Your Reservoir

Dopamine neurons fire at a tonic rate of approximately 3-4 times per second. This is your baseline — the background hum of motivation and wellbeing. When baseline is healthy, you feel capable, interested, and willing to engage with the world. When baseline drops, everything feels harder and less rewarding.

Baseline is not fixed. It shifts based on your recent history of peaks and troughs. This is what makes dopamine management so consequential: what you did yesterday and last week is shaping how motivated you feel right now.

> "Your previous levels of dopamine are influencing your levels of dopamine right now, and your current levels of dopamine and where you take them next will influence your dopamine levels in the next days and weeks to come." — Andrew Huberman

### Peaks: The Triggers

Different activities and substances produce peaks of varying magnitude above baseline:

| Trigger | Approximate Increase Above Baseline |
|---------|-------------------------------------|
| Chocolate | ~1.5x (50% increase) |
| Food anticipation | ~2x (100% increase) |
| Exercise (if enjoyed) | ~2x (100% increase) |
| Nicotine | ~2.5x (150% increase) |
| Cocaine | ~10x (1,000% increase) |
| Amphetamine/methamphetamine | 10x-100x (1,000%-10,000% increase) |

These are population averages derived from neuroimaging, PET scans, blood draws, and animal studies. Individual variability is enormous. The subjective enjoyment of an activity meaningfully affects the dopamine response — exercise, for instance, varies widely depending on whether the person likes the specific form of exercise.

### Troughs: The Crash

Every peak is followed by a trough that drops **below** baseline. The depth and duration of the trough is proportional to the height and steepness of the peak. A moderate peak from a satisfying meal produces a mild, brief trough. A massive spike from amphetamine produces a deep, prolonged trough.

The trough is experienced as pain, craving, restlessness, or amotivation. It is the neurochemical substrate of "the comedown." And here is the key: the trough is not just the absence of pleasure — it is an active state of discomfort that drives further pursuit.

> "If you continue to indulge in the same behaviors or even different behaviors that increase your dopamine in these big peaks over and over and over again, we won't experience the same level of joy from those behaviors or from anything at all." — Andrew Huberman

### The Depletion Problem

Repeatedly spiking dopamine depletes the readily releasable pool of dopamine vesicles. This lowers baseline over time. The result: you need bigger peaks to feel the same level of motivation, while your resting state becomes progressively more flat and anhedonic. This is the neurochemical architecture of tolerance and addiction — but it applies to non-pathological behaviors as well.

Time is the primary requirement for replenishing dopamine stores. The neurons must synthesize new dopamine from tyrosine, package it into vesicles, and transport it to release sites. There is no shortcut for this process.

### Protecting Baseline

The practical implication: guard your baseline. Strategies include:

1. Avoid stacking multiple dopamine triggers on the same activity
2. Use dopamine-enhancing supplements intermittently, not daily
3. Maintain the four foundational practices (sleep, morning sunlight, exercise, tyrosine-rich nutrition)
4. Allow time between high-dopamine experiences for the pool to refill
5. Practice finding reward in effort itself (see growth mindset dopamine reframe)

## Example

A software engineer notices that her deep work sessions have become progressively less satisfying over the past month. She audits her routine and discovers she has been stacking pre-workout caffeine + L-tyrosine + energizing music + a sugary snack every morning before coding. Each session produces a high peak, followed by an afternoon crash. Over weeks, her baseline has dropped — she now needs the full stack just to feel normal, and coding without it feels pointless.

She applies the baseline-peak framework: she drops all supplements except baseline caffeine, removes music from work sessions 4 out of 5 days, and replaces the sugary snack with a protein-rich meal. For two weeks, her sessions feel harder. Then her baseline recovers, and she begins enjoying the work again without the full enhancement stack.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

- Identify whether a current motivation problem is a baseline issue (chronic depletion) or a trough issue (acute post-peak drop)
- Estimate the relative dopamine impact of your daily behaviors and substances
- Design a daily routine that protects baseline rather than chasing peaks
- Recognize when you are in a trough and make the decision to wait it out rather than chase another peak
