## When to Use

Use this when someone has already implemented morning sunlight and wants to strengthen their circadian system further, or when someone asks about evening routines, protecting against the effects of artificial light at night, or building a complete light-based sleep protocol. This is the second anchor point in Huberman's two-anchor circadian system (morning light = first anchor, evening light = second anchor).

## The Protocol

### The Core Practice

In the late afternoon or evening, as the sun descends toward the horizon, get sunlight in your eyes for 5-30 minutes. The same condition-dependent duration rules as the morning protocol apply:

| Condition | Duration |
|-----------|----------|
| Clear evening sky | 5-10 minutes |
| Cloudy evening | 10-15 minutes |
| Overcast | 20-30 minutes |

Face the general direction of the sun. The same safety rules apply — do not stare at any light source that causes pain or discomfort. Blink naturally.

### Why This Works: The Second Oscillator

The circadian system does not run on a single clock signal. Morning light and evening light activate different sets of oscillators in the suprachiasmatic nucleus. The wavelength composition of light shifts across the day: morning light features a strong yellow-blue contrast, while evening light shifts toward yellow-blue-orange-red wavelengths. The brain uses this spectral shift as a "time of day" signal.

> "Getting that sunlight in your eyes in the late afternoon and evening signals to that clock that it's evening time and that sleep is coming. It also serves as a second anchor or reference point for your body and your brain to know where it is in time." — Andrew Huberman, h2aWYjSA1Jc.md

With two anchor points (morning and evening), the circadian clock is more stable and more resistant to disruption from artificial light, irregular schedules, and other perturbations.

### The Inoculation Effect

This is the most practically valuable aspect of sunset viewing. Evening sunlight exposure partially protects the circadian system against the negative effects of artificial light later that night.

> "There's a great study which showed that if people view evening sunset light or evening sunsets or sunlight right around the time of sunset, it really serves to inoculate or offset some, again, some, not all, of the negative effects of artificial light at night." — Andrew Huberman, h2aWYjSA1Jc.md

This does not mean you can blast overhead lights all evening and cancel the damage with a sunset viewing. The inoculation is partial, not complete. But for people who inevitably use screens, watch television, or spend time in lit environments after dark, the evening sunlight practice reduces the circadian disruption from that exposure.

### Practical Implementation

1. Check your local sunset time (weather app, Google).
2. Aim to be outside starting 30-60 minutes before sunset and through sunset.
3. A walk is ideal — combines the light exposure with light exercise.
4. If you cannot be outside, a window with a clear western view provides partial benefit (less ideal than outdoors, but the low-angle spectral composition still registers).
5. Combine with the Evening Light Restriction Protocol: after sunset viewing, transition to dim, low-positioned indoor lights.

### The Complete Two-Anchor Day

| Time | Action | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| Within 30-60 min of waking | Morning sunlight (5-30 min) | Set circadian clock, trigger cortisol pulse |
| Late afternoon/evening | Sunset viewing (5-30 min) | Set second anchor, inoculate against night light |
| After sunset | Dim, low-positioned lights | Protect melatonin onset |
| Bedtime | Complete darkness | Enable sleep architecture |

### Caveats

- This protocol partially offsets, but does NOT fully eliminate, the negative effects of bright artificial light at night. It is a buffer, not a shield.
- The same safety rules as morning viewing apply: do not look at any light that causes pain.
- In winter at high latitudes, sunset may occur during work hours. Even 5-10 minutes of outdoor exposure during the last light of the day provides benefit.
- If you can only do one (morning or evening), choose morning. The morning anchor is more powerful for setting the circadian clock. Evening viewing is an enhancement, not a replacement.

## Example

A family in Colorado implements a "sunset walk" tradition. Each evening, 30 minutes before sunset, they walk the neighborhood for 20-30 minutes. The parents notice they feel sleepier earlier in the evening. They transition to dimmed indoor lights after returning. After two weeks, the children fall asleep 15-20 minutes faster, and the parents report fewer instances of lying awake after screen use.

## Output

After implementing this protocol, a person should have:
- A specific daily time for evening light exposure based on their local sunset
- Understanding of the two-anchor circadian model (morning + evening)
- Realistic expectations about the partial protection against artificial light
- A complete light protocol from morning through bedtime
- Knowledge that morning sunlight is the priority if they can only do one
