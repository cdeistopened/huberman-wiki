## When to Use

Use this when someone asks about cardiovascular health, how much cardio they need, steady-state cardio, heart health, cerebrovascular health, Zone 2 training, or fat oxidation during exercise. Zone 2 cardio is one of the most frequently cited protocols across Huberman Lab, appearing in the foundational fitness template, the Galpin series, and multiple solo episodes. Huberman considers it the non-negotiable baseline of cardiovascular fitness.

## The Protocol

Zone 2 cardio is sustained, low-to-moderate intensity movement that keeps the heart rate elevated enough to build cardiovascular and cerebrovascular health, but low enough that you can maintain it for long durations without fatigue or glycolytic burnout. It builds capillary beds, mitochondrial density, and metabolic efficiency — the infrastructure that supports all other forms of exercise.

### Step 1: Determine Your Zone 2 Intensity

Zone 2 corresponds to roughly 55-70% of maximum heart rate, or 40-60% of VO2 max. But you do not need a heart rate monitor. Huberman provides two field tests that are more practical:

**The Nasal Breathing Test:** If you can breathe purely through your nose — inhaling and exhaling only through the nose — during the activity, you are in or near Zone 2. The moment you need to open your mouth to get enough air, you have exceeded it.

**The Conversation Test:** You should be able to hold a conversation without gasping or needing to pause mid-sentence to catch your breath. If you can speak in full sentences comfortably, you are in Zone 2. If you can only manage short phrases, you are above it.

> "Zone 2 cardio is the type of movement that we typically call cardio exercise that elevates your heart rate somewhat, increases your breathing somewhat, but that still allows you to carry out a conversation without having to pause or to gasp in order to complete your sentences." — Andrew Huberman, LYYyQcAJZfk.md

### Step 2: Accumulate 150-200 Minutes Per Week

The minimum effective dose is 150 minutes per week. Huberman sets the target at 200 minutes per week for most people seeking optimal cardiovascular and cerebrovascular health.

> "The scientific data tell us that we should all be getting anywhere from 150 minutes to 200 minutes per week minimum of zone 2 cardio for sake of cardiovascular health, cerebrovascular health." — Andrew Huberman, LYYyQcAJZfk.md

This volume does not need to be achieved in dedicated workout sessions. It can be accumulated across the week through any combination of:

- Dedicated sessions: 60-90 minute jog, hike, or bike ride (1-2x per week)
- Daily movement: brisk walking, taking stairs, walking while on phone calls
- Active commuting: cycling, walking to errands

Dr. Andy Galpin's key insight, which Huberman endorses: Zone 2 is not really "exercise" in the traditional sense. It is baseline daily movement that modern sedentary life has eliminated. If you are meshing it into daily activities (walking, stairs, errands), you likely do not need to count minutes — the volume accumulates naturally.

### Step 3: Choose Appropriate Activities

Any activity that keeps heart rate in the Zone 2 range qualifies:

| Activity | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| Brisk walking | Easiest entry point; may need hills or a weighted vest for fitter individuals |
| Hiking | Natural terrain variation keeps intensity appropriate |
| Light jogging | Most common dedicated session format |
| Cycling | Low impact; good for those with joint issues |
| Swimming | Full-body; harder to monitor breathing cues |
| Rowing | Engages upper and lower body |

### Step 4: Separate Zone 2 From Higher-Intensity Cardio

Zone 2 is a distinct training modality. It is not your only cardiovascular training — it is the base layer. In Huberman's foundational fitness protocol, Zone 2 coexists with moderate-intensity cardio sessions (25-30 min) and high-intensity interval training. These are separate adaptations.

> "Zone 2 cardio does not impede, and in fact, can enhance our other aspects of fitness. So for example, our strength training, our hypertrophy training, or any type of speed work." — Andrew Huberman, LYYyQcAJZfk.md

### Hydration During Zone 2

For sessions lasting more than 15 minutes, use the Galpin Equation: body weight in pounds divided by 30 equals the ounces of water to consume every 15 minutes of exercise. A 180-pound person would drink 6 oz every 15 minutes.

### Caveats

- A fitness tracker is useful but not required. The nasal breathing and conversation tests are sufficient for most people.
- Zone 2 does not replace dedicated cardiovascular training sessions (HIIT, tempo work, etc.).
- If you are very fit, brisk walking may not reach Zone 2 — you may need to jog, use incline, or add a weighted vest.
- If you are deconditioned, even slow walking may put you above Zone 2. Scale back pace until you can maintain nasal breathing.

## Example

A 45-year-old software engineer works from home and averages 2,500 steps per day. He has no dedicated exercise routine. Rather than committing to gym sessions immediately, he begins with Zone 2 accumulation: a 30-minute brisk walk each morning (nasal breathing only), walking during all phone calls (approximately 40 minutes per day), and a 60-minute hike on Saturday mornings. Within the first week, he is accumulating 200+ minutes of Zone 2 without any "workouts." After 4-6 weeks, he notices improved energy, lower resting heart rate, and the ability to walk faster while still maintaining nasal breathing — a sign of improved aerobic capacity.

## Output

After 4-8 weeks of consistent Zone 2 accumulation (150-200 min/week), expect:
- Lower resting heart rate (improved cardiovascular efficiency)
- Ability to sustain higher pace/effort while remaining in Zone 2
- Improved cerebrovascular health (blood flow to the brain)
- Enhanced recovery capacity between higher-intensity training sessions
- Improved fat oxidation at rest and during low-intensity activity
- Foundation for layering in higher-intensity cardiovascular and resistance training
