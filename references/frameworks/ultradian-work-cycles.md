# Ultradian Work Cycles

## The Protocol

Work in **90-minute focused blocks** aligned with your body's natural ultradian rhythms. After each block, take a **10–20 minute genuine break**. Limit yourself to **2–3 deep focus blocks per day.**

## The Biology: Ultradian Rhythms

Your body runs on cycles within cycles. The best known is the **circadian rhythm** (24-hour cycle), but nested within it are **ultradian rhythms** — approximately 90-minute cycles that govern fluctuations in alertness, attention, and cognitive capacity throughout the day.

During sleep, these manifest as 90-minute sleep cycles (cycling through stages). During wakefulness, they manifest as waves of heightened and reduced focus capacity.

This is not metaphorical. EEG studies show that neural oscillation patterns shift approximately every 90 minutes during wakefulness, moving between states of higher and lower cortical excitability.

## The Anatomy of a 90-Minute Block

### Minutes 0–10: The Ramp-Up

The first 5–10 minutes of a focus block are the hardest. Your brain is transitioning from a diffuse attention state into a focused one. This feels like resistance — the urge to check your phone, get another coffee, switch tasks.

**This is normal and expected.** Huberman emphasizes: push through this phase. It's not a sign that you can't focus today — it's the neurological cost of entering deep focus. Acetylcholine (the neurotransmitter of focused attention) takes time to reach effective levels at the synapses involved in your task.

### Minutes 10–60: Peak Focus

Once through the ramp-up, you enter the productive core of the block. Acetylcholine is elevated, norepinephrine is providing alertness, and dopamine is maintaining your motivation to continue.

This is where deep work happens — writing, coding, problem-solving, learning new material, creative synthesis.

### Minutes 60–90: The Taper

After roughly 60 minutes, focus quality begins to decline. Adenosine is accumulating, acetylcholine is depleting, and neural fatigue is setting in. You can still work, but error rates increase and creative output diminishes.

Don't fight this phase — use it for lower-demand activities within the same project (organizing notes, reviewing what you've written, outlining next steps). Pushing for high-quality output past 90 minutes produces diminishing returns.

## The Break: 10–20 Minutes

After 90 minutes, take a genuine break. "Genuine" means:

- **Non-visual rest if possible:** Close your eyes, look at a distance, go for a walk outside. Staring at a different screen is not a break for the visual system.
- **No demanding cognitive input:** Don't use the break to consume news, social media, or dense reading. Your brain needs time to shift from focused to diffuse processing.
- **Movement helps:** Walking, stretching, or light activity during the break accelerates the reset.
- **NSDR counts:** A 10-minute NSDR protocol during the break is an excellent option.

The break allows your brain to enter a **diffuse mode** where it processes and consolidates the work you just did. Many people report that solutions to problems they were stuck on arrive during or just after a break.

## Stacking Tools with the 90-Minute Block

### Caffeine Timing

If you're using caffeine, time it so peak blood levels coincide with the productive core of your focus block. Caffeine reaches peak blood concentration about **30–45 minutes after ingestion.**

Practical approach: drink your coffee ~30 minutes before starting the block, or at the very beginning of the block. By the time you're through the ramp-up phase, caffeine is at peak effect.

### Visual Focus Trigger

Huberman describes a technique to accelerate the ramp-up phase: **narrow your visual gaze to a single point** (a spot on the screen, a point on the wall, the tip of your pen) for **30–60 seconds** at the start of the block.

The mechanism: focused visual attention and focused cognitive attention share neural circuitry. Narrowing your visual field activates the sympathetic nervous system and the brain regions involved in concentrated attention. It's a physical on-ramp to mental focus.

This is the opposite of panoramic vision (which activates the parasympathetic system and is calming — useful for the break).

### Binaural Beats / Brown Noise

Some people find 40 Hz binaural beats or brown noise helpful during the focus block. Huberman has discussed the evidence for 40 Hz stimulation increasing gamma wave activity (associated with focus and information binding). The evidence is preliminary but the practice is low-risk.

## Capacity Limits

Huberman is clear: **2–3 deep focus blocks per day is the realistic maximum** for most people. That's 3–4.5 hours of truly deep work.

The remaining work hours are for:
- Meetings, emails, and administrative tasks
- Shallow work that doesn't require deep focus
- Exercise, meals, social interaction
- Recovery (which fuels tomorrow's deep work)

Trying to do 6+ hours of deep focus daily leads to burnout, not productivity. The quality of each block matters more than the number of blocks.

## Caveats

- The 90-minute figure is an average. Individual ultradian cycles range from 80–120 minutes. Pay attention to your own focus decay patterns.
- Some tasks (creative writing, flow-state activities) may sustain focus beyond 90 minutes. If you're genuinely in flow, don't interrupt yourself because a timer went off. The protocol is a guideline, not a prison.
- ADHD and other attention-related conditions may significantly alter these dynamics. Work with a clinician if focus is persistently impaired.
- Sleep quality from the previous night is the single biggest determinant of focus capacity. No amount of protocol optimization overcomes chronic sleep deprivation.
