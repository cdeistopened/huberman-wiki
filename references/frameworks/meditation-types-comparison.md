## When to Use

Use this when someone asks about meditation, wants to start a meditation practice, or has tried meditation and found it unhelpful or counterproductive. Huberman's key insight is that meditation is not one thing — different types produce different neurological effects, and the wrong type for a given person can make their condition worse. An anxious person doing interoceptive (eyes-closed, body-focused) meditation may intensify their anxiety. A dissociated, checked-out person doing the same practice may finally access what they need. This framework helps match the person to the practice.

## The Protocol

### Step 1: The Interoception-Exteroception Self-Assessment

Before choosing a meditation type, determine where you fall on the interoception-exteroception continuum:

**Interoceptively biased (too much internal focus):**
- You are highly aware of your heartbeat, breathing, gut sensations
- You tend to ruminate, overthink, and spiral into emotional states
- You may have anxiety, panic, or hypochondria tendencies
- You already "live inside your head" — more internal focus makes it worse

**Exteroceptively biased (too much external focus):**
- You feel disconnected from your body and emotions
- You tend to dissociate, check out, or go numb under stress
- You may struggle to identify what you are feeling
- You are "checked out" — and need to reconnect with internal signals

> "If you're somebody who has a tremendous amount of interoceptive awareness, well, then meditating on your internal state may not be good, and actually, there's some evidence that it may actually be bad." — Andrew Huberman, wTBSGgbIvsY.md

### Step 2: Choose the Opposite Practice

The principle: meditate in the direction you are NOT already biased toward.

**If interoceptively biased → Exteroceptive meditation:**
- Eyes open
- Focus on an external point (candle flame, spot on the wall, distant object)
- Attention directed outward, away from body sensations
- When attention drifts inward, gently redirect it outward

**If exteroceptively biased → Interoceptive meditation:**
- Eyes closed
- Focus on the "third eye" center (point between the eyebrows) or the breath
- Attention directed inward, toward body sensations
- When attention drifts outward, gently redirect it inward

> "Meditation tends to be a focusing practice. Along those lines, a meditation practice that is one that is exteroceptively biased, where you focus on things that are outside your body, can be wonderful for somebody who tends to focus too much on their inner landscape." — Andrew Huberman, wTBSGgbIvsY.md

### Step 3: The Three Major Types

**Type 1: Focused Attention Meditation**

Concentrate on a single point — breath, mantra, external object. When attention wanders, notice and return. This is essentially a focus-training exercise for the prefrontal cortex.

- Best for: improving concentration, reducing mind-wandering, building attentional control
- Duration: 13 minutes per day minimum (see below)
- Resource: Waking Up app (Sam Harris), recommended by Huberman

**Type 2: Open Monitoring Meditation**

Sit with eyes closed (or softly open). Do not focus on anything specific. Instead, observe whatever arises — thoughts, sensations, sounds — without engaging or following any of them. The practice is noticing without reacting.

- Best for: reducing emotional reactivity, developing equanimity, breaking rumination patterns
- Duration: 13 minutes per day minimum
- More advanced than focused attention — typically easier after establishing a focused attention practice first

**Type 3: Loving-Kindness / Compassion Meditation**

Generate feelings of warmth and goodwill toward yourself and others. Often structured in expanding circles: self → loved ones → neutral people → difficult people → all beings.

- Best for: social anxiety, self-criticism, depression, improving relationships
- Activates pro-social neural circuits (serotonin-mediated, anterior cingulate cortex)
- Can be combined with the gratitude practice protocol for compounding effects

### Step 4: The 13-Minute Threshold

Research by Dr. Wendy Suzuki at NYU found that 13 minutes per day of guided meditation produced measurable improvements in:
- Attention and focus
- Working memory
- Mood and emotion regulation
- These benefits appeared after consistent daily practice

This is the minimum effective dose supported by the literature Huberman cites. Less than 13 minutes may still help, but the data is strongest at this threshold.

### The Science

> "A Wandering Mind is an Unhappy Mind" — Killingsworth & Gilbert, *Science* (cited by Huberman, wTBSGgbIvsY.md)

Key findings Huberman synthesizes across episodes:
- Mind-wandering correlates with unhappiness regardless of the activity being performed
- Meditation reduces default mode network activity (the "mind-wandering network")
- 13 min/day meditation: improved attention, memory, mood, emotion regulation (Wendy Suzuki, NYU)
- Two 20-minute sessions per day may reduce total sleep need (debated but reported)
- Meditation reduces cortisol and shifts the default mode network away from rumination
- The interoceptive-dissociative continuum is shaped like a bowl (healthy), flat (functional), or convex/mountain (pathologic)
- Sleep deprivation makes the continuum more convex (pathologic — narrower range of healthy response)

### Meditation vs. NSDR

Meditation and NSDR (Non-Sleep Deep Rest) are not the same practice and serve different purposes:

| Feature | Meditation | NSDR / Yoga Nidra |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| Attention | Focused (sustaining concentration) | Deliberately defocused |
| Purpose | Train attention, improve mood, reduce reactivity | Replenish dopamine, recover from sleep loss |
| Neural effect | Default mode network adjustment | Striatal dopamine increase |
| Best time | Any time EXCEPT close to bedtime | Upon waking, after poor sleep, midday |
| Minimum dose | 13 min/day | 10-30 min |

### Caveats

- Meditating close to bedtime can cause insomnia — meditation is a focus practice, not a relaxation practice
- The wrong type of meditation can worsen anxiety (interoceptive meditation for someone already interoceptively biased)
- Consistency matters more than duration — 13 minutes daily beats 60 minutes once a week
- Meditation is not NSDR. NSDR is for rest and dopamine replenishment. Meditation is for attention training and mood.
- Some people find meditation aversive at first — this is normal and typically resolves within 2-3 weeks of consistent practice

## Example

A 28-year-old woman with generalized anxiety tries meditation apps but finds that sitting still with her eyes closed and focusing on her breath makes her anxiety spike. Using this framework, she identifies herself as interoceptively biased — she is already hyper-aware of her heartbeat, breathing, and body tension. She switches to an exteroceptive meditation: eyes open, focused on a candle flame on her desk, 13 minutes per day. Within 2 weeks, she notices her ability to disengage from anxious thought loops has improved, and the meditation itself no longer triggers the anxiety it once did.

## Output

After 2-4 weeks of daily meditation practice (13 min/day, correct type for the individual), a person should experience:
- Improved ability to sustain attention on chosen tasks
- Reduced frequency and intensity of mind-wandering
- Better emotion regulation — not suppression, but faster recovery from emotional disruption
- Improved working memory performance
- For the anxiety-prone: reduced tendency to spiral into interoceptive loops
- For the dissociated: improved body awareness and emotional connection
