## When to Use

Apply this framework when someone asks about taking melatonin for sleep, whether melatonin is safe, giving melatonin to children, melatonin dosage, or alternatives to melatonin. Also apply when someone mentions they are currently taking melatonin nightly and wants to evaluate that practice.

## The Core Position: Generally Avoid Chronic Melatonin Supplementation

Huberman and Dr. Matthew Walker both advise against routine melatonin use based on five concerns:

### 1. Supraphysiological Dosing

Commercial melatonin products typically contain 1-20mg per dose. The body naturally produces far less than 1mg per night. Standard supplements deliver 10-200x more melatonin than the body would ever produce on its own.

> "The optimal doses, where you do get sleep benefits in the populations we've looked at, are somewhere between 0.1 and 0.3 milligrams of melatonin. In other words, the typical doses are usually 10 times, 20 times, maybe more than what your body would naturally expect." — Dr. Matthew Walker, JaRGJVrJBQ8.md

### 2. Melatonin Is a Timing Signal, Not a Sleep Generator

Melatonin tells the body "it is nighttime" — it signals the onset of the biological night. But it does not produce sleep itself.

> "Melatonin is like the starting official at the 100-meter race in the Olympics. It calls all of the sleep racers to the line and it begins the great sleep race, but it doesn't participate in the race itself." — Dr. Matthew Walker, JaRGJVrJBQ8.md

### 3. Minimal Efficacy for Healthy Adults

Meta-analysis of melatonin studies shows it increases total sleep time by only 3.9 minutes on average and improves sleep efficiency by only 2.2%. For most healthy adults, the effect is barely distinguishable from placebo.

### 4. Hormone System Interactions

Melatonin is not merely a sleep signal. It interacts with:
- **Testosterone** — exogenous melatonin can affect testosterone levels
- **Estrogen** — melatonin modulates estrogen pathways
- **Puberty-related hormones** — particularly concerning for children and adolescents

> "The dosages of melatonin that are contained in most commercial products is far, far, far greater than what we would make endogenously. So it's really supraphysiological... melatonin is not just responsible for making us sleepy and fall asleep. It also does things like interacts with other hormone systems, testosterone and estrogen, even in the puberty system, in kids." — Huberman

### 5. Especially Concerning for Children

Chronic melatonin use in children may affect pubertal development. The widespread practice of giving children nightly melatonin gummies is one of Huberman's specific concerns.

> "Taking melatonin every once in a while... for adjusting to jet lag? Probably not [a problem]. I would even say no. But taking it chronically over time, especially kids taking it chronically over time, can potentially be problematic." — Andrew Huberman, h2aWYjSA1Jc.md

## The Exceptions

### Jet Lag (Occasional Use)

Both Huberman and Walker consider occasional melatonin use for jet lag to be low-risk and potentially helpful. The dose should be 0.1-0.3mg (100-300 micrograms) — far below what is commercially standard. This is short-term, targeted use — fundamentally different from nightly supplementation.

### Older Adults (65+)

Adults 65 and older may benefit from melatonin supplementation because the pineal gland calcifies with age, reducing natural melatonin production. This is the one population where chronic supplementation has a physiological rationale.

## Dosage

| Scenario | Dose | Frequency |
|----------|------|-----------|
| Jet lag adjustment | 0.1-0.3mg (100-300mcg) | Occasional (travel only) |
| Adults 65+ | 0.1-0.3mg (100-300mcg) | Nightly (physician-guided) |
| Standard commercial dose | 1-20mg | NOT recommended |
| Children | Not recommended | Not recommended |

## The Regulation Problem

Melatonin is classified as a dietary supplement in the United States, not a drug. It is not subject to FDA dosage accuracy requirements. Studies have found that actual melatonin content in commercial products can vary from 83% less to 478% more than what is listed on the label. Some products have been found to contain serotonin as a contaminant.

## What to Use Instead

Huberman's preferred sleep supplement stack (see Sleep Supplement Stack framework):

| Supplement | Dosage | Timing |
|------------|--------|--------|
| Magnesium L-Threonate | 145mg | 30-60 min before bed, nightly |
| Apigenin | 50mg | 30-60 min before bed, nightly |
| L-Theanine | 100-400mg | 30-60 min before bed, nightly (if tolerated) |

These work through different mechanisms (GABA modulation, chloride channels, relaxation) without the hormonal concerns of melatonin. Behavioral tools (morning sunlight, evening light restriction, temperature regulation, sleep consistency) should be the first line of intervention.

## Transition Path for Current Users

If someone is currently taking melatonin nightly: introduce the sleep stack components one at a time, confirm they work, then taper off melatonin. Abrupt discontinuation may cause temporary rebound insomnia.

## Caveats

- **Most people do not realize melatonin is a hormone.** It is marketed alongside vitamins and minerals, but it acts as a hormone with systemic effects beyond sleep onset.
- **"Natural" does not mean "safe at any dose."** Melatonin is naturally produced by the body, but so are cortisol, testosterone, and estrogen. Supplementing any hormone at supraphysiological doses has consequences.
- **Even "low-dose" products (0.5-1mg) exceed physiological levels.** The optimal supplemental dose is 0.1-0.3mg.
- **Label accuracy is unreliable.** Actual content can vary by 83% less to 478% more than labeled.

## Example

A parent gives their 8-year-old a 3mg melatonin gummy every night because the child resists bedtime. After learning about the hormone system concerns and the supraphysiological dosing, they switch to: consistent bedtime routine, dimmed lights 1 hour before bed, room temperature lowered to 67F, and no screens in the bedroom. The melatonin is discontinued. Sleep onset takes 2-3 days to adjust, then stabilizes without supplementation.

## Output

When addressing melatonin use, always specify:
1. That Huberman recommends against chronic melatonin supplementation
2. The supraphysiological dosage problem (commercial doses 10-200x natural production)
3. The hormone interaction concerns (testosterone, estrogen, puberty)
4. The specific concern about children taking melatonin chronically
5. The two exceptions: occasional jet lag use, and adults 65+
6. The correct dose if used at all: 0.1-0.3mg (not the 1-20mg in commercial products)
7. The sleep stack (magnesium threonate 145mg + apigenin 50mg + theanine 100-400mg) as the recommended alternative
