## When to Use

Use this when someone asks about screens before bed, blue light glasses, overhead lights at night, sleeping with lights on, or reports difficulty falling asleep despite being tired. This protocol addresses the asymmetry in how the brain responds to light: it takes massive amounts of light to wake up in the morning, but very little light to disrupt the circadian clock at night.

## The Protocol

### The Core Rule: Avoid Bright Artificial Light Between 10pm and 4am

> "The diabolical twist in the way that your brain and body respond to light is that early in the day, in the morning hours, you need a lot of bright light to be very alert and to wake up, but in the evening hours and nighttime hours, it takes very little light, very few photons, in order to wake up your brain and body and to disrupt your circadian clock." — Andrew Huberman, h2aWYjSA1Jc.md

This is not just about blue light. Bright light of ALL colors disrupts the circadian system at night. Blue-blocking glasses help at the margins, but brightness matters far more than wavelength.

### Step 1: Dim Indoor Lights After Sunset

Once the sun goes down, dim indoor lights as much as possible. The goal is to create an environment that approximates the light levels the brain evolved to experience after dark.

| Light Source | Lux Level | Effect at Night |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Bright sunny day | ~100,000 lux | N/A (daytime) |
| Cloudy day | ~10,000 lux | N/A (daytime) |
| Bright artificial light | ~1,000 lux | Highly disruptive |
| Typical room light | ~100-200 lux | Too bright at night |
| Candlelight | ~3-10 lux | Safe — does not reset circadian clock |
| Moonlight | ~0.1-0.3 lux | Safe |

### Step 2: Use Low-Positioned Lights, Not Overhead

This is the critical detail most people miss. Melanopsin ganglion cells — the cells that signal the circadian clock — sit predominantly in the lower half of the retina. The lower retina views the upper visual field. This means overhead lights are especially disruptive at night because they hit the most sensitive part of the retinal circadian system.

Use desk lamps, floor lamps, and table lamps instead of overhead fixtures. Place light sources at or below eye level whenever possible.

### Step 3: Screens — Dim as Low as Possible

If watching TV or using a computer in the evening, reduce screen brightness to the lowest comfortable setting. Phone night modes and blue-light filters help somewhat, but reducing overall brightness is more important.

### Step 4: Bathroom and Night Waking

If you must get up at night (bathroom, children), use red light or the dimmest light source available. A red nightlight in the bathroom minimizes circadian disruption.

Note: commercial "red light therapy" panels are typically far too bright and would wake up the brain despite being red. A dim red nightlight is not the same thing as a bright red therapy panel.

### Step 5: Sleep in Complete Darkness

> "Even having a small amount of light in the room, which isn't even that bright, while you're sleeping with eyes closed can disrupt morning glucose levels." — Andrew Huberman, CJIXbibQ0jI.md

A PNAS study found that 100 lux of overhead light during sleep — even with eyes closed — elevated morning blood glucose compared to sleeping in 1-10 lux. Light penetrates the eyelids. Use blackout curtains or an eye mask (keeping in mind the temperature consideration — eye masks can trap heat on the upper face).

### The 6-8 Hour Dark Window (Mental Health Dimension)

Beyond sleep quality, maintaining 6-8 continuous hours of dim-to-dark environment every 24-hour cycle has independent mental health benefits. A Nature Mental Health study of 85,000+ subjects found that darkness at night reduced risk across bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, depression, and PTSD — and these effects were independent of both daytime light exposure and sleep quality.

> "People who stay in very dim to dark environments for eight hours every 24 hours... benefit from improved mood and mental health outcomes in a way that's independent of how much light they're getting and independent of how much sleep they are getting." — Andrew Huberman, CJIXbibQ0jI.md

### What About Blue-Light Blocking Glasses?

Huberman does not consider them harmful but views them as insufficient alone. The primary variable is brightness, not wavelength. Blue blockers on top of a 1,000-lux overhead light are still far too much light at night. Blue blockers in a dimly lit room with low-positioned lamps are a reasonable addition.

### What Is Safe at Night

Candlelight, fireplace light, and moonlight do NOT reset the circadian clock (confirmed across multiple episodes). These light sources fall in the 0.1-10 lux range, which is below the threshold for melanopsin activation. Dinner by candlelight is circadian-friendly.

## Example

A couple watches TV from 8-10pm with all overhead lights on (roughly 200-300 lux combined with screen). They switch to: overhead lights off after sunset, one floor lamp behind the couch on its dimmest setting, TV brightness reduced 50%. They use a dim red nightlight in the bathroom. In the bedroom, they install blackout curtains. Sleep onset improves by 20-30 minutes within the first week.

## Output

After applying this protocol, a person should have:
- A specific plan for which lights to use after sunset (low, dim, floor/desk level)
- An understanding that brightness matters more than blue-light filtering
- Blackout conditions in the bedroom
- A red or dim light solution for nighttime bathroom visits
- Knowledge that 6-8 hours of darkness has independent mental health benefits beyond sleep
