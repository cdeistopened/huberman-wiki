## When to Use

Apply this framework when someone asks about caffeine timing, the 90-minute caffeine delay rule, how caffeine affects sleep, afternoon energy crashes, caffeine and cortisol, or optimal coffee consumption patterns. Also relevant when someone reports afternoon fatigue, poor sleep despite good habits, or wants to understand the adenosine mechanism behind caffeine.

## The Core Rule: Delay 90-120 Minutes After Waking

> "I highly recommend that everybody delay their caffeine intake for 90 to 120 minutes after waking... you want to clear out whatever residual adenosine is circulating in your system in that first 90 to 120 minutes of the day." — Andrew Huberman, h2aWYjSA1Jc.md

### Why This Works: The Adenosine Mechanism

Caffeine is an adenosine receptor antagonist. Adenosine is a molecule that accumulates in the brain during waking hours and creates the sensation of sleepiness (sleep pressure). Caffeine does not eliminate adenosine — it blocks the receptors so you cannot feel it. The adenosine continues to build behind the caffeine "wall."

When caffeine wears off, all the accumulated adenosine hits at once. Huberman describes this as a "tsunami wave" — the built-up adenosine plus the adenosine that continued accumulating while caffeine was active. This is the afternoon crash.

By delaying caffeine 90-120 minutes, you allow the body to clear residual adenosine from sleep naturally. The cortisol pulse from waking (ideally triggered by morning sunlight) handles the first 90 minutes of alertness. Then caffeine extends alertness through the day with a much shallower crash.

But Huberman is candid that he does not always follow this rule himself.

> "I've tried to be clear about this in previous episodes, but I think a number of people have come to think that I always delay my caffeine intake 90 to 120 minutes after waking, and that's simply not the case. If I'm going to train early in the day, I do ingest caffeine prior to training." — Andrew Huberman, LYYyQcAJZfk.md

The rule is a useful guideline, not a rigid protocol. On training days when Huberman exercises early, he drinks caffeine before the workout regardless of timing. The delay is most valuable on non-training days when sustained alertness through the afternoon matters more.

### The Adenosine Mechanism (Simplified Steps)

1. During sleep, adenosine is cleared from the brain
2. Upon waking, adenosine levels are low and begin rising
3. Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors (masks sleepiness, does not remove adenosine)
4. If consumed immediately upon waking, caffeine blocks receptors while adenosine is already low
5. When caffeine wears off (5-6 hours later), accumulated adenosine floods unblocked receptors
6. Result: afternoon crash
7. Delaying caffeine allows natural adenosine clearance first, producing smoother sustained alertness

### The Afternoon Cutoff

Cut caffeine 8-10 hours before bedtime. For most people, this means no later than approximately 2pm.

> "For those people who keep drinking into the evening... the depth of their deep sleep is not as deep anymore. And it can be up to 30%. And for me to drop your deep sleep by 30%, I'd have to age you by between [significant years]." — Dr. Matthew Walker, JaRGJVrJBQ8.md

Late-day caffeine reduces deep sleep quality by up to 30%, according to Dr. Matthew Walker's research. Even if you can fall asleep after afternoon caffeine, your sleep architecture — particularly deep sleep — suffers.

### The Cortisol Connection

For chronic caffeine users, caffeine does not significantly spike cortisol. But it does extend the duration of cortisol's presence in the bloodstream. This is actually useful: the morning cortisol peak drives alertness and immune function, and caffeine prolongs it.

> "If you're somebody who regularly drinks caffeine... it's not going to boost your cortisol levels that much... However, it will prolong the duration of cortisol's effectiveness in your bloodstream." — Huberman

For non-habitual caffeine users, the cortisol spike from caffeine is significant and potentially uncomfortable. This distinction matters when advising someone who does not regularly consume caffeine.

When you drink caffeine immediately upon waking, you are "spending" the natural cortisol pulse through caffeine rather than allowing the body to use cortisol alone for the first phase of alertness. This creates dependency: the body learns to rely on caffeine for morning wakefulness rather than its own cortisol system. Delaying caffeine preserves and extends the natural cortisol arc.

## Dosage & Timing

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Dosage** | Individual; most people use 100-400mg. Huberman personally consumes ~800+ mg/day |
| **Preferred source** | Yerba mate (Huberman's choice), coffee, tea |
| **Morning delay** | 90-120 minutes after waking (guideline, not rigid rule) |
| **Exercise exception** | Early morning training — caffeine before workout is fine regardless of timing |
| **Afternoon cutoff** | 8-10 hours before bedtime (for most people, no later than ~2pm) |

### Genetic Variation

Some people are genetically fast caffeine metabolizers (CYP1A2 gene variants) and may tolerate later caffeine without sleep disruption. However, Huberman and Walker note that even fast metabolizers often underestimate caffeine's impact on deep sleep — the subjective feeling of "falling asleep fine" does not mean sleep architecture is preserved.

### Step-by-Step Implementation

1. Wake up. Get morning sunlight (see Morning Sunlight Protocol).
2. Drink water. Do light movement or exercise if desired.
3. At the 90-120 minute mark after waking, have your first caffeine.
4. If exercising early, caffeine before the workout is acceptable — just know the afternoon dip may be stronger.
5. Stop all caffeine 8-10 hours before bed (typically by 2pm).
6. If you need afternoon energy, use NSDR (10-20 min) rather than more caffeine.

## Example

A software engineer drinks a large coffee at 6:30am (immediately upon waking), another at 10am, and a third at 3pm. He crashes hard at 4pm and struggles to fall asleep before midnight. He shifts to: morning sunlight at 6:30am, water and a walk until 8:30am, first coffee at 8:30am, second coffee at 11am, no caffeine after noon. Within a week, the afternoon crash is substantially reduced, and he falls asleep by 10:30pm.

## Caveats

- The 90-120 minute delay is a useful guideline but not a rigid rule. Huberman himself breaks it on training days. Do not treat it as dogma.
- Chronic caffeine users experience a different cortisol response than occasional users. Habitual users get less of a spike but a longer duration; non-habitual users get a sharp spike.
- Afternoon caffeine (after approximately 2pm for someone who sleeps at 10pm) disrupts sleep architecture even when it does not prevent sleep onset. Deep sleep and REM are affected.
- Huberman's personal consumption (~800+ mg/day) is well above typical recommendations. He has built substantial tolerance over years. This is not a target to aim for.
- Yerba mate is Huberman's preferred source partly because it contains additional compounds (GLP-1 effects on satiety) beyond caffeine itself.
