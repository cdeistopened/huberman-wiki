## When to Use

Use this when someone asks about breathing techniques, breathwork, or wants to know which breathing protocol fits their situation. Huberman has discussed at least five distinct breathing protocols across many episodes, each with a different neurological effect. The wrong protocol at the wrong time can make things worse — cyclic hyperventilation before bed would be counterproductive, and box breathing during a panic attack is slower than necessary. This guide maps each protocol to its correct use case.

## The Protocol

Huberman's lab conducted a study comparing four practices head-to-head: cyclic sighing, box breathing, cyclic hyperventilation, and meditation. Cyclic sighing was the most effective at reducing resting heart rate and improving mood when measured 24 hours later. But "most effective for calming" does not mean "best in every situation."

### Protocol 1: Physiological Sigh (Real-Time, 5 Seconds)

**What it does:** Fastest voluntary method to shift from sympathetic to parasympathetic activation.

**The technique:**
1. Deep inhale through the nose
2. Second short inhale through the nose at the top (reinflates collapsed alveoli)
3. Long extended exhale through the mouth

**Duration:** ~5 seconds per rep. 1-3 reps is sufficient.

**When to use:** Acute stress moments, panic, pre-presentation, between exercise sets, mid-conversation. Any time you need to calm down in the next 10 seconds.

**When NOT to use:** Not a sustained practice. For ongoing calming, use cyclic sighing instead.

> "If you do one truly, just one physiological sigh at the beginning of the rest period, you are going to effectively shift your nervous system in the direction you want it to go during those rest periods." — Andrew Huberman, LYYyQcAJZfk.md

### Protocol 2: Cyclic Sighing (Calming, 5 Minutes)

**What it does:** Sustained parasympathetic activation. Reduces resting heart rate and improves mood for 24+ hours.

**The technique:**
1. Sit or lie comfortably
2. Perform repeated physiological sighs (double inhale nose, long exhale mouth)
3. Continue for 5 minutes
4. All exhales should be longer and more vigorous than inhales

**Duration:** 5 minutes. Post-workout: 3-5 minutes.

**When to use:** Post-workout cool-down, evening wind-down, chronic stress management, daily practice for mood improvement. This is the protocol that won Huberman's head-to-head study.

**When NOT to use:** Not during exercise sets (only during rest periods or after the workout).

> "You could do a repeated round of physiological sighs. So the double inhale through the nose, long exhale through the mouth but repeated for say 3 minutes. That's another version." — Andrew Huberman, LYYyQcAJZfk.md

### Protocol 3: Box Breathing (Neutral/Balancing, 5-10 Minutes)

**What it does:** Balances sympathetic and parasympathetic systems. Creates calm alertness rather than pure calming.

**The technique:**
1. Inhale through the nose for a count of 4-6 seconds
2. Hold lungs full for 4-6 seconds
3. Exhale through the mouth for 4-6 seconds
4. Hold lungs empty for 4-6 seconds
5. Repeat

**Duration:** 5-10 minutes.

**When to use:** When you need calm focus rather than pure relaxation. Pre-task preparation, transitions between activities, situations requiring steady composure. Used by Navy SEALs for sustained performance under pressure.

**When NOT to use:** Less effective than cyclic sighing for pure stress reduction (per the head-to-head study). Not the best choice for acute panic.

### Protocol 4: Cyclic Hyperventilation / Wim Hof-Style (Alertness + Stress Inoculation, 5 Minutes)

**What it does:** Deliberately raises sympathetic arousal. Increases adrenaline and alertness. Builds stress tolerance over time.

**The technique:**
1. Inhale deeply through the nose, exhale through the mouth — rapid pace
2. Continue vigorous breathing for 25-30 breaths
3. On the last exhale, hold breath with lungs empty for 25-60 seconds
4. Resume and repeat until 5 minutes total
5. Daily practice for approximately 2 weeks as an intervention

**Duration:** 5 minutes per session. 2-week intervention period.

**When to use:** Morning alertness, deliberate stress inoculation training, building resilience to future stressors. This is intentionally uncomfortable.

**When NOT to use:** Before bed. During acute anxiety or panic. People with panic disorder or severe anxiety should avoid this entirely or use only with clinician guidance.

> "It involves basically doing this for five minutes which is hyperventilating... but not continuously for the five minutes because many people would pass out or feel extremely uncomfortable." — Andrew Huberman, tpntW9Tte4M.md

### Protocol 5: Exhale-Emphasized Breathing (Gentle Calming, Open Duration)

**What it does:** Mild parasympathetic shift without the structured format of cyclic sighing.

**The technique:**
1. Breathe normally through the nose
2. Make each exhale slightly longer than each inhale (e.g., 4-count inhale, 6-count exhale)
3. Continue for any duration

**Duration:** Any length. Can be maintained during other activities.

**When to use:** Background calming during work, while walking, while reading. A gentler version of the calming protocols for people who find cyclic sighing too structured.

### Decision Matrix

| Situation | Protocol | Duration |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| Acute panic or stress spike | Physiological Sigh | 5 sec, 1-3 reps |
| Post-workout cool-down | Cyclic Sighing | 3-5 min |
| Daily stress reduction practice | Cyclic Sighing | 5 min |
| Pre-task focus and composure | Box Breathing | 5-10 min |
| Morning alertness boost | Cyclic Hyperventilation | 5 min |
| Building long-term stress tolerance | Cyclic Hyperventilation | 5 min/day, 2 weeks |
| Background calming during work | Exhale-Emphasized | Open-ended |
| Between exercise sets | Physiological Sigh | 1 rep |

### Caveats

- Cyclic hyperventilation is the only protocol here that deliberately increases stress. All others reduce it.
- People with anxiety or panic disorders should start with calming protocols only.
- Exhale-emphasis = calming. Inhale-emphasis = alertness. This is the fundamental principle underlying all of these protocols.
- None of these replace clinical treatment for anxiety disorders, but they can meaningfully complement it.

## Example

A college student has an exam at 2pm. At 8am, she does 5 minutes of cyclic hyperventilation to sharpen morning alertness. At 1:45pm, she does 5 minutes of box breathing to create calm focus. During the exam, when she hits a question that triggers anxiety, she does one physiological sigh before continuing. After the exam, she walks home using exhale-emphasized breathing to bring herself back to baseline.

## Output

After selecting and applying the correct breathing protocol, a person should experience:
- Appropriate nervous system state for the task at hand (calm, alert, focused, or resilient)
- Measurable heart rate changes within 1-5 minutes (depending on protocol)
- No conflicting effects (e.g., no accidental alertness when trying to calm down)
- A repeatable toolkit they can deploy throughout the day as situations shift
