## When to Use

Apply this framework when a user asks about a contested health claim, when Huberman's position contradicts mainstream guidelines, or when evaluating supplements/protocols with mixed evidence. It separates what is scientifically established from what is politically or commercially motivated.

## The Truth Types (Adapted for Health)

Balaji Srinivasan distinguishes between types of truth based on how they're established. In health and nutrition, these distinctions matter because much of what people "know" about health comes from political consensus or commercial marketing, not from controlled experiments.

| Truth Type | How It's Established | Health Example | Reliability |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------|-------------|
| **Scientific Truth** | Controlled experiment, replication, peer review | "Cold exposure increases norepinephrine 2-3x" (measured in RCTs) | Highest |
| **Political Truth** | Consensus of institutions, guidelines committees | "Dietary cholesterol raises heart disease risk" (FDA/AHA guidelines, now revised) | Variable — depends on era and incentive structure |
| **Social Truth** | What your peer group believes, repeated enough to feel true | "Breakfast is the most important meal of the day" (cereal marketing from 1944) | Low — survives by repetition, not evidence |
| **Commercial Truth** | What a company needs you to believe to buy their product | "You need 8 glasses of water per day" (bottled water marketing) | Low — follows money, not data |

### Claims Where Commercial Truth Drives Belief

| Commercial Claim | Who Benefits | Scientific Reality |
|-----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| "You need a multivitamin daily" | Supplement industry ($50B/yr) | Huberman recommends specific, targeted supplementation based on deficiency, not broad multivitamins. |
| "Pre-workout supplements are essential" | Supplement companies | Caffeine (1-3mg/kg) is the only performance-enhancing ingredient with robust evidence. Most pre-workout ingredients are underdosed. |
| "Protein timing matters (anabolic window)" | Protein powder marketing | "The 30-minute anabolic window is largely a myth. Total daily protein intake matters far more than timing." |

## How to Apply

When evaluating any health claim:

1. **Identify the truth type.** Who established this claim? A controlled experiment, a government committee, your gym buddy, or a company selling something?
2. **Check the incentive structure.** Does anyone profit from you believing this? (Commercial truth is the most common source of health misinformation.)
3. **Look for Huberman's hedge language.** When he says "the data are clear" vs. "it's possible" vs. "many people report" - he's implicitly classifying evidence quality.
4. **Separate the mechanism from the claim.** A plausible biological mechanism (scientific truth) doesn't guarantee the health claim built on top of it (which may be commercial or social truth).

## The Nutrition Minefield

Nutrition is the domain where truth types collide most violently. Huberman acknowledges this repeatedly:

> "Nutrition is incredibly contentious because the quality of studies is often poor, the variables are hard to control, and there are enormous financial interests at play."

This is why Huberman's supplement recommendations come with specific caveats about cycling, blood work, and individual variation. He treats most nutrition claims as provisional, not settled.
