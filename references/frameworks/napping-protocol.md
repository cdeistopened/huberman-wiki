## When to Use

Use this when someone asks whether they should nap, reports post-nap grogginess, wonders if napping hurts their nighttime sleep, or wants to optimize an existing napping habit. The answer depends heavily on whether the person struggles with insomnia — napping is beneficial for good sleepers and counterproductive for insomnia sufferers.

## The Protocol

### The Decision Tree: Should You Nap?

**Nap if:**
- You sleep well at night and nap regularly without nighttime disruption
- You have an unusual sleep deficit (bad night, early flight, newborn)
- You need performance for a specific afternoon/evening task

**Do NOT nap if:**
- You struggle with insomnia or difficulty falling asleep at night
- You are trying to build stronger sleep pressure for nighttime sleep
- You have never napped regularly (it is not required)

> "For people with insomnia, we typically advise against napping... if you can nap regularly and you don't struggle with sleep at night, then naps are just fine." — Dr. Matthew Walker, JaRGJVrJBQ8.md

### Duration: The Three Options

| Duration | Type | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|------|
| 17-25 minutes | Power nap | Avoids deep sleep inertia, rapid alertness boost | Does not include REM or deep sleep |
| ~90 minutes | Full cycle | Includes both non-REM and REM, wakes at the top of a cycle | Takes longer, may reduce nighttime sleep pressure |
| 26 minutes | NASA standard | Proven: 34% performance improvement, 50% alertness improvement | Minimal |

> "Naps of as little as 26 minutes improved mission performance by 34% and improved daytime alertness by 50%." — Dr. Matthew Walker, JaRGJVrJBQ8.md (citing NASA research from the 1990s)

The ideal short nap is 20-25 minutes. This keeps you in lighter sleep stages (stages 1-2) and avoids the deep sleep (stage 3-4) that causes grogginess upon waking. If you regularly wake from naps feeling worse than before, your nap is too long — you are entering deep sleep and waking mid-cycle.

> "If you do take a nap and you don't want to have that grogginess hangover... try to limit it to about 20 or 25 minutes." — Dr. Matthew Walker, JaRGJVrJBQ8.md

### Timing: The Cutoff Rule

Cut off naps at least 6-8 hours before your regular bedtime. Napping too late in the day releases sleep pressure (adenosine) that you need for nighttime sleep onset. For someone who sleeps at 10pm, the nap cutoff is 2-4pm.

### The NSDR Alternative

If naps make you groggy or interfere with nighttime sleep, NSDR (Non-Sleep Deep Rest) provides many of the same restoration benefits without entering actual sleep. A 10-20 minute NSDR session restores alertness, and because you remain technically awake, it does not release the adenosine sleep pressure that napping does. This makes it a better option for:
- People with insomnia
- People who wake from naps feeling worse
- People who need afternoon restoration after 4pm (when napping would be too late)

### Implementation Steps

1. Set an alarm for 25 minutes (allows 5 minutes to fall asleep + 20 minutes of sleep).
2. Lie down in a comfortable, dimly lit space. An eye mask helps.
3. If you do not fall asleep within 10 minutes, switch to an NSDR script instead.
4. When the alarm goes off, get up immediately. Do not snooze.
5. Bright light exposure after waking from the nap helps clear residual grogginess.
6. If you consistently cannot fall asleep for naps, you do not need them — not everyone is a napper, and that is fine.

### Naps as Short as 17 Minutes Show Benefits

Walker's research shows that naps as brief as 17 minutes produce measurable improvements in learning and memory. The common belief that naps must be 30+ minutes to be useful is incorrect. Shorter is often better because it avoids deep sleep inertia entirely.

## Example

A sales executive has a 3pm client presentation. She slept poorly the night before (5.5 hours instead of her usual 7.5). At 12:30pm, she sets an alarm for 12:55pm, lies down with an eye mask, and falls asleep within 5 minutes. The 20-minute nap keeps her in light sleep. She wakes feeling refreshed, has a glass of water, and steps outside for 5 minutes of sunlight to clear any residual grogginess. Her 3pm performance is markedly better than it would have been without the nap.

## Output

After applying this protocol, a person should know:
- Whether they should nap at all (based on their insomnia status)
- The optimal nap duration for their situation (20-25 min for most people)
- The latest time they can nap without disrupting nighttime sleep
- When to use NSDR instead of a nap
- How to avoid post-nap grogginess (shorter duration, bright light after)
