## When to Use

Use this when someone reports trouble falling asleep, waking up hot in the middle of the night, or asks about room temperature, cooling mattresses, hot showers before bed, or the relationship between body temperature and sleep. Temperature is a gate: if the body does not cool down, sleep cannot occur, regardless of how tired the person feels.

## The Protocol

### The Core Principle: Your Body Must Drop 1-3 Degrees F to Sleep

> "If your body does not drop by one to three degrees, you are simply not going to get into deep sleep or stay in deep sleep." — Andrew Huberman, h2aWYjSA1Jc.md

This is not a preference — it is a physiological requirement. The 1-3 degree Fahrenheit drop in core body temperature is what allows the brain to transition into and sustain deep sleep. Conversely, an increase of 1-3 degrees triggers waking.

### Step 1: Cool the Room

Drop your room temperature by at least 3 degrees Fahrenheit from its daytime setting. Most people sleep best between 65-68F (18-20C), though individual variation exists. The key is that the sleeping environment should be noticeably cooler than the daytime environment.

### Step 2: Use the Blanket-Extremity Method

Use blankets for comfort, but leverage your body's natural heat exchange portals when you get too warm.

> "If you lower the temperature in your sleeping environment... it's naturally going to make your sleep environment cooler, and if you're too warm under the blankets, all you have to do is extend a hand or a foot out from under those blankets." — Andrew Huberman, h2aWYjSA1Jc.md

**Glabrous skin portals** (the body's radiator surfaces, identified by Dr. Craig Heller at Stanford):
- Palms of the hands
- Bottoms of the feet
- Upper half of the face

These areas contain specialized blood vessel structures (arteriovenous anastomoses) that rapidly dump heat from the body's core. Sticking a hand or foot out from under the blankets activates this cooling system.

### Step 3: The Sock Question

- If you tend to wake up overheated: do NOT wear socks. Socks block the foot glabrous skin portal, preventing heat dissipation.
- If you tend to wake up cold: wear socks to retain heat through the foot portal.

### Step 4: The Hot Shower/Bath Paradox

A hot bath, hot shower, or sauna session in the evening (20-30 minutes maximum) triggers a paradoxical cooling response. When you exit the heat, the medial preoptic area of the hypothalamus overcorrects, dropping core body temperature by 1-3 degrees — exactly the drop needed for sleep onset.

> "If you do a hot tub or a hot bath or a sauna in the evening, and you don't stay in for more than 20 or 30 minutes and you get out... there's a compensatory cooling off of your core body temperature... and it will make it much easier to get into sleep." — Andrew Huberman, h2aWYjSA1Jc.md

After the hot bath/shower, follow with a cool-ish or warm rinse. Do NOT take a cold shower — cold water triggers a compensatory heating response (the opposite of what you want at night).

### Step 5: Optional — Cooling Mattress Technology

Cooling mattress covers (e.g., Eight Sleep) can programmatically lower the sleep surface temperature. Huberman uses and endorses this approach, though he notes it is not required — room temperature and the blanket method work for most people.

### The Temperature Minimum

The lowest point in your body temperature cycle occurs approximately 2 hours before your typical wake-up time. This is called the temperature minimum. After this point, body temperature rises, eventually triggering wakefulness. Understanding this timing is critical for the Jet Lag Protocol (see separate framework) and for understanding why you naturally wake at a consistent time each day.

### What NOT to Do

- Do not take a cold shower before bed. Cold exposure triggers adrenaline and dopamine release, raises core body temperature through compensatory heating, and will wake you up.
- Do not overstay in a hot bath/sauna (beyond 30 minutes). You may overheat rather than trigger the compensatory cooling response.
- Do not use an eye mask that traps heat over the upper face (a glabrous skin area) unless the room is cool enough to compensate.

## Example

A couple keeps their bedroom at 72F. One partner sleeps hot and wakes up at 2am nightly; the other sleeps cold. They drop the thermostat to 67F. The hot sleeper removes socks and extends one foot from the blankets. The cold sleeper adds socks and an extra blanket. Both take a warm (not scalding) shower 30 minutes before bed. The hot sleeper's 2am wakings stop within 3 nights. The cold sleeper sleeps through unchanged.

## Output

After applying this protocol, a person should have:
- A specific room temperature setting for their bedroom
- A strategy for managing heat through the night (blankets, extremities, socks)
- Knowledge of whether a hot shower before bed would help them
- Understanding of why cold showers at night are counterproductive
- The temperature minimum concept for understanding their circadian rhythm
