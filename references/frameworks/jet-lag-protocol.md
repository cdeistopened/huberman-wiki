## When to Use

Use this when someone is traveling across time zones, starting shift work, or trying to deliberately shift their sleep-wake schedule earlier or later. The temperature minimum method is Huberman's core framework for intentional circadian clock shifting — it works for jet lag, shift work, and voluntary schedule changes.

## The Protocol

### Step 1: Calculate Your Temperature Minimum

Your temperature minimum is the lowest point in your core body temperature cycle. It occurs approximately 2 hours before your typical wake-up time.

> "Your temperature minimum is not a temperature. It's a time within your 24-hour cycle. Approximately two hours before your typical wake-up time, your body is at its lowest temperature that it will ever be in the 24-hour cycle." — Andrew Huberman, h2aWYjSA1Jc.md

| If You Normally Wake At | Your Temperature Minimum Is Approximately |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| 5:00am | 3:00am |
| 6:00am | 4:00am |
| 7:00am | 5:00am |
| 8:00am | 6:00am |
| 9:00am | 7:00am |

### Step 2: Use the Phase Advance / Phase Delay Rules

Four signals shift the circadian clock: bright light, exercise, caffeine, and food. Their effect depends entirely on whether they occur before or after your temperature minimum.

**To Phase ADVANCE (Fall Asleep and Wake Earlier)**
- View bright light, exercise, drink caffeine, or eat in the hours AFTER your temperature minimum
- Example: if temp min is at 5am, doing any of these between 5:30am-8am will shift your clock earlier

**To Phase DELAY (Fall Asleep and Wake Later)**
- View bright light, exercise, drink caffeine, or eat in the 2-4 hours BEFORE your temperature minimum
- Example: if temp min is at 5am, doing any of these between 1am-4am will shift your clock later

### Step 3: Apply to Jet Lag

**Traveling East (Need to Phase Advance)**

You need to fall asleep and wake earlier relative to your home time zone.

1. Calculate what time it is in your home time zone when you arrive.
2. Seek bright light exposure after your home-time-zone temperature minimum.
3. Avoid bright light before your home-time-zone temperature minimum (this would shift you the wrong direction).

> "When you land Italy, you want to avoid light like the plague... because otherwise it's going to delay you. It's going to send you to California instead of sending you to Italy." — Dr. Samer Hattar, 2XMb3MD6g6Q.md

Pre-travel preparation: start waking 30 minutes earlier per day for several days before departure.

**Traveling West (Need to Phase Delay)**

You need to fall asleep and wake later relative to your home time zone.

1. Seek bright light exposure in the hours before your home-time-zone temperature minimum (which now falls at a convenient evening time in your destination).
2. Stay up later at your destination — this aligns with the delay direction.

Pre-travel preparation: start staying up 30 minutes later per day for several days before departure.

### Step 4: Eat on the Local Schedule

One of the fastest ways to shift the circadian clock is to eat meals at local mealtimes upon arrival. The gut has its own circadian system (peripheral clocks) that feeds back to the master clock in the suprachiasmatic nucleus. Eating breakfast at local breakfast time, even if you are not hungry, accelerates adjustment.

### Step 5: Use Melatonin Sparingly (If At All)

For jet lag specifically, occasional melatonin at 0.1-0.3mg (100-300 micrograms) is considered low-risk by both Huberman and Walker. Take it at the desired bedtime in your new time zone. This is the one use case where Huberman conditionally supports melatonin.

### The Critical Mistake to Avoid

Getting the light signal wrong can shift your clock in the opposite direction. If you arrive in Europe at 8am local time, but your body thinks it is 2am (before your temperature minimum), bright light will DELAY your clock — sending you further from your goal. In this scenario, you should avoid bright light for the first few hours and seek it once your biological clock passes the temperature minimum.

### Shift Work Application

Shift workers face the same circadian physics as jet lag travelers but without the advantage of a consistent destination time zone. Huberman's recommendations:
- Try to stay on the same shift for at least 2 weeks minimum (swing shifts are very harmful).
- Use red light if you must be awake during your biological night — red light minimally disrupts the cortisol rhythm.
- On days off, try to maintain the shifted schedule rather than reverting to a "normal" schedule and back.

### Timeline for Full Adjustment

Expect the body to shift approximately 1-1.5 hours per day with aggressive light/food/exercise timing. A 6-hour time zone difference takes roughly 4-6 days for full adjustment. Be patient — forcing it with excessive caffeine or sleep deprivation creates a worse outcome.

## Example

A woman flies from San Francisco (PST) to London (GMT, +8 hours). She normally wakes at 7am PST, so her temperature minimum is approximately 5am PST (which is 1pm in London). She arrives in London at 10am local time. Her body thinks it is 2am. She avoids bright outdoor light for the first 3 hours (until her body passes its temperature minimum at 1pm London time). At 1pm London time, she goes outside for 30 minutes of bright sunlight. She eats lunch on the local schedule. She takes 0.2mg melatonin at 10pm London time. By day 3, she is sleeping 11pm-7am London time with minimal grogginess.

## Output

After applying this protocol, a traveler should have:
- Their calculated temperature minimum in both home and destination time zones
- A specific plan for when to seek and avoid bright light in the first 48 hours
- A meal timing strategy aligned with the destination
- Realistic expectations for adjustment timeline (1-1.5 hours per day)
- Knowledge of the critical mistake (wrong-direction light exposure) and how to avoid it
