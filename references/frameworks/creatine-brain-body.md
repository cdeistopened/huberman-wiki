## When to Use

Apply this framework when someone asks about creatine supplementation, whether creatine is worth taking, creatine for brain function or cognitive performance, which form of creatine to use, or how to dose creatine based on body weight. Also relevant when someone asks about supplements for depression, bipolar disorder, or neurodegenerative conditions where creatine has clinical data.

## The Recommendation

Creatine monohydrate is one of the most well-studied and broadly beneficial supplements available. Huberman considers it foundational -- useful for physical performance, cognitive function, and emerging mental health applications. The phosphocreatine system fuels both muscle cells and brain neurons, which is why creatine's benefits span both domains.

The key insight most people miss: the universal "5 grams per day" recommendation is not well-informed. Dosage should be scaled to body weight. Huberman, at approximately 220 lbs, takes 10-15 grams per day and reports benefits at that higher dosage for both physical and cognitive performance.

There is no need to "load" creatine (the old protocol of 20g/day for a week). Consistent daily intake reaches saturation over time. And creatine monohydrate is the only form worth buying -- Huberman finds no evidence that other forms (creatine HCl, buffered creatine, creatine ethyl ester) are superior.

> "If you weigh 185 pounds or so... 10 to 15g of creatine per day is probably more appropriate for you than is 5 grams, meaning it's going to be more effective for enhancing physical performance. And perhaps... even cognitive performance as well." -- Huberman

> "I see no evidence whatsoever that the other forms of creatine are superior to creatine monohydrate." -- Huberman

Dr. Chris Palmer adds the mental health dimension with clinical evidence:

> "We do have randomized controlled trial data with creatine showing that it can improve symptoms of major depression, it can augment antidepressants, it can improve symptoms of bipolar disorder, and it can improve symptoms of neurodegenerative disorders like cognition in Alzheimer's disease." -- Dr. Chris Palmer

## Dosage & Timing

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Form** | Creatine monohydrate only (least expensive, most studied, most effective) |
| **Dosage (130-180 lbs)** | 3-5 grams per day |
| **Dosage (185-250 lbs)** | 10-15 grams per day |
| **Timing** | Does not matter -- pre-workout, post-workout, or any time of day |
| **With food** | Some people need to take with food to avoid GI distress |
| **Loading phase** | Not needed; daily consistency reaches saturation |
| **Cycling** | Not needed; continuous daily use |

## Caveats

- Some people experience GI distress, particularly at higher doses. Start at the low end and increase gradually. Taking with food can help.
- Creatine is found only in animal-sourced foods. Vegans and vegetarians tend to have lower baseline creatine levels and may see more pronounced cognitive benefits from supplementation.
- Huberman has explicitly addressed the claim that creatine causes hair loss: there is no evidence supporting this.
- Creatine causes water retention in muscles, which increases body weight on the scale. This is not fat gain.
- Huberman considers creatine safe enough even for those under 18 (with parental guidance), though this is a minority position.
- The mental health applications (depression, bipolar, neurodegeneration) are supported by randomized controlled trial data per Dr. Chris Palmer, but should be discussed with a psychiatrist before use in that context.

## Output

When recommending creatine, always specify:
1. Creatine monohydrate as the only form worth taking
2. Body-weight-adjusted dosage (not the generic 5g recommendation)
3. That timing and loading are irrelevant -- just take it consistently
4. Both physical and cognitive benefits as reasons to consider it
5. The GI tolerance issue and food pairing as a solution
