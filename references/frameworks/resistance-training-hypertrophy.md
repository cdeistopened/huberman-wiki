## When to Use

Use this when someone asks about building muscle, how many sets and reps they should do, rest periods between sets, minimum effective volume, the difference between strength and hypertrophy training, or how to structure resistance training for body composition goals. This protocol synthesizes Huberman's recommendations drawn from the Andy Galpin series, the Pavel Tsatsouline episode, and the Science of Muscle Growth episode.

## The Protocol

Resistance training produces two distinct adaptations — hypertrophy (muscle size) and strength (force production) — and the training parameters that optimize each are different. Huberman emphasizes that the load range for hypertrophy is far wider than most people assume, and that the ability to isolate and contract a muscle determines how efficiently you stimulate it.

### Hypertrophy: Sets, Reps, and Volume

**Load range:** 30-80% of one-rep max. This is a much wider range than the traditional "8-12 reps" recommendation. The critical variable is not the weight — it is whether you bring the set close to or to failure.

**Weekly volume per muscle group:**

| Volume | Outcome |
|--------|---------|
| 5 sets/week | Maintenance minimum — prevents muscle loss |
| 10-15 sets/week | Growth range for most people |
| 20-30 sets/week | Advanced trainees only; risk of overtraining |

> "Five sets per week in this 30% to 80% of the one-repetition maximum range is what's required just to maintain your muscle." — Andrew Huberman, jgaoLdS82vw.md

**Failure and intensity:** About 10% of total sets should be taken to true muscular failure (cannot complete another rep with proper form). The remaining 90% should be taken to near-failure — typically 1-2 reps in reserve. Training every set to failure accumulates excessive fatigue without proportional benefit.

**Mind-muscle connection:** For hypertrophy specifically, the ability to isolate and deliberately contract the target muscle is a significant efficiency factor. A person who can generate a strong contraction in the target muscle will need fewer total sets to achieve the same growth stimulus.

> "The better you are at contracting particular muscles and in isolating those muscles, the fewer sets likely you need to do in order to get the desired effect." — Andrew Huberman, jgaoLdS82vw.md

**Rest periods for hypertrophy:** 60-120 seconds between sets when training for metabolic stress and hypertrophy. Shorter rest increases metabolic byproduct accumulation (lactate, hydrogen ions), which contributes to the hypertrophic signaling cascade.

### Strength: The 3-by-5 Protocol

For pure strength development (neural adaptations, force production), Huberman recommends the 3-by-5 protocol derived from Dr. Andy Galpin:

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Exercises per workout | 3-5 |
| Sets per exercise | 3-5 |
| Reps per set | 3-5 |
| Rest between sets | 3-5 minutes |
| Training frequency | 3-5 days per week |
| Block duration | 8-12 weeks |

> "He used this 3 by 5 protocol... It involves doing three to five exercises per workout... for 3 to 5 sets per exercise, 3 to 5 repetitions per set, and 3 to 5 minutes of rest between each set." — Andrew Huberman, LYYyQcAJZfk.md

The longer rest periods (3-5 minutes) are essential for strength work because the phosphocreatine system needs full recovery between heavy sets. Cutting rest periods short converts the session into a metabolic/hypertrophy stimulus rather than a neural/strength stimulus.

**Warm-up for strength work:** Progress through 2-3 warm-up sets at ascending loads (e.g., 6-8 reps at light weight, then 4-6 reps at moderate weight, then 2-4 reps approaching work weight) before beginning work sets.

**Small muscle groups:** For muscles like rear delts, calves, and neck, use 5-8 reps instead of 3-5. These muscles respond better to slightly higher rep ranges.

### Strength vs. Hypertrophy: The Key Distinction

- **Strength** = moving progressively heavier loads through compound movements (squats, deadlifts, presses, rows). Focus on total system force production.
- **Hypertrophy** = generating isolated contractions in target muscles. Focus on the quality of each rep and the mind-muscle connection.

Both adaptations contribute to the other, but the training emphasis and rest periods differ substantially. Most people benefit from including both in their programming — strength blocks (8-12 weeks) alternating with or alongside hypertrophy-focused work.

### Age-Related Decline Rates (Without Training)

After age 40, untrained individuals lose:
- Muscle size: ~1% per year
- Strength and power: 3-5% per year
- Speed and explosiveness: 8-10% per year

Resistance training offsets all three decline curves. The minimum to maintain existing muscle is 6-10 working sets per muscle group per week.

### Caveats

- People who can generate very high force per contraction may need fewer total sets.
- Excessively high volume (30+ sets/week per muscle) is counterproductive for most people.
- Allow 48-72 hours between sessions targeting the same muscle group.
- Cold water immersion after resistance training blunts hypertrophy — see the cold-after-training-caveat protocol.
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen, etc.) also blunt adaptations; avoid within 4 hours before or after training.

## Example

A 38-year-old woman wants to build muscle in her legs and shoulders while maintaining overall strength. She trains 4 days per week. On leg day and shoulder day, she uses the hypertrophy approach: 3-4 exercises per muscle group, 3 sets each at 60-75% 1RM, taken to 1-2 reps from failure, with 90-second rest periods. This gives her 9-12 sets per muscle group per session, hitting the 10-15 set/week growth range. On her two other training days, she does compound strength work (squat, deadlift, bench press) using the 3-by-5 approach with 3-5 minute rest periods. Over 12 weeks, she increases her squat by 15% and adds visible muscle to her quads and shoulders.

## Output

A well-structured resistance training program should produce:
- Measurable strength increases within 4-6 weeks (neural adaptations occur first)
- Visible hypertrophy within 8-12 weeks (muscle protein synthesis takes longer)
- Maintained or improved body composition
- Protection against age-related muscle and strength decline
- A clear weekly volume target (sets per muscle group) that can be tracked and adjusted
