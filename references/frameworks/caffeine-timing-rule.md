# Caffeine Timing Rule

## The Protocol

Delay caffeine intake **90–120 minutes after waking**. Hard cutoff: no caffeine within **8–10 hours of bedtime**.

## Why: The Adenosine Problem

Adenosine is a molecule that builds up in your brain while you're awake. It binds to adenosine receptors, creating the feeling of sleepiness. During sleep, adenosine is cleared.

When you wake up, you still have residual adenosine in your system. It takes roughly 90–120 minutes of wakefulness for your body to clear most of this residual adenosine through natural processes (cortisol pulse, movement, light exposure).

Caffeine works by **blocking** adenosine receptors — it doesn't eliminate adenosine. If you drink caffeine immediately upon waking:

1. You mask the residual adenosine rather than clearing it
2. When the caffeine wears off (4–6 hours later), that unclearned adenosine is still there — plus the new adenosine that accumulated during the day
3. Result: the **afternoon crash** that most people attribute to "needing more coffee"

By waiting 90–120 minutes, you allow the natural adenosine clearance to happen first. When you then drink caffeine, you're blocking *new* adenosine accumulation rather than masking old buildup. The afternoon crash largely disappears.

## The Morning Cortisol Pulse

Waking triggers a natural cortisol pulse — cortisol rises 50% or more in the first 30–45 minutes after waking (the cortisol awakening response). This is healthy and desirable. It promotes alertness and immune function.

Caffeine amplifies cortisol release. Drinking caffeine during the natural cortisol peak creates an unnecessarily high spike followed by a steeper crash. Waiting allows the cortisol pulse to do its job, then caffeine extends the alertness plateau.

## The Exception: Early Morning Exercise

If you train first thing in the morning, caffeine before your workout is acceptable. The rationale:

- Exercise itself clears adenosine aggressively
- The performance benefit of caffeine for training is well-established
- The physical activity drives its own cortisol and epinephrine response

In this case, you're using exercise to accomplish what the 90-minute wait would have done. Huberman has noted he follows this exception himself on training days.

## The Afternoon Cutoff

Even if you "sleep fine" with late caffeine, it's damaging your sleep architecture. Caffeine consumed within 8–10 hours of sleep has been shown to:

- **Reduce deep sleep (slow-wave sleep) by up to 20%**
- Increase time in lighter sleep stages
- Reduce the restorative quality of sleep even when total sleep duration is unchanged

You won't feel this as "bad sleep" — you'll feel it as reduced energy, worse mood, and impaired learning the next day. Then you reach for more caffeine earlier, deepening the cycle.

Huberman's recommendation: if you sleep at 10 PM, last caffeine by noon–2 PM. Adjust accordingly for your schedule.

## Practical Implementation

| 6:00 AM   | 7:30–8:00 AM      | 12:00–2:00 PM                 |
| 7:00 AM   | 8:30–9:00 AM      | 12:00–2:00 PM                 |

## Dosage Notes

Huberman typically references **1–3 mg caffeine per kg of body weight** as an effective dose. For a 180 lb (82 kg) person, that's roughly 80–250 mg — one strong cup of coffee to two cups.

## Caveats

- Genetic variation in caffeine metabolism (CYP1A2 gene) means some people clear caffeine faster. The 8–10 hour rule is conservative but safe for everyone.
- If you have anxiety, caffeine on an empty stomach can exacerbate it regardless of timing.
- Pregnant or breastfeeding individuals should consult a physician about caffeine limits.
